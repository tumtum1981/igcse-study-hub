Phloem and Translocation

Structure of Phloem

Phloem is the vascular tissue that transports sugars (mainly sucrose) and other organic compounds around the plant. Unlike xylem, phloem consists of living cells.

Phloem is made up of two main cell types:

Cell Type Features Function
Sieve tube elements Living cells; thin walls; large pores in end walls called sieve plates; very little cytoplasm; no nucleus at maturity Form the main channels through which sucrose solution flows
Companion cells Living cells with a nucleus and many mitochondria; closely associated with sieve tube elements Support the sieve tubes — provide energy (ATP) via respiration for the active loading and unloading of sugars

Translocation

Translocation is the transport of sucrose (and other solutes such as amino acids) through the phloem from sources to sinks.

  • Source: where sucrose is produced or released into the phloem (e.g. photosynthesising leaves)
  • Sink: where sucrose is used or stored (e.g. roots, growing shoot tips, fruits, storage organs like potato tubers)
  • Sucrose flows from areas of high concentration (source) to areas of low concentration (sink)
  • The direction of transport can be up or down the plant — this is called bidirectional flow
KEY CONCEPT

Source → Phloem → Sink
Sucrose is actively loaded into phloem at the source (using ATP from companion cells) and unloaded at the sink. This maintains a concentration gradient that drives flow.

Evidence for Translocation

The ringing experiment (bark ringing) provides evidence that phloem is responsible for translocation:

  • A ring of bark (containing phloem) is removed from a tree trunk
  • The tissue above the ring swells up — sugars accumulate because they cannot pass downward
  • The tissue below the ring does not swell — no sugar reaching it
  • This shows sugars are transported in the phloem (which is in the bark), not in the wood (which contains xylem)

Additional evidence: the use of radioactive tracers (14C-labelled CO₂) shows that labelled sugars produced in leaves appear in the phloem, confirming translocation occurs through phloem.

Xylem vs Phloem — Comparison

Feature Xylem Phloem
Cell typeDead cellsLiving cells
Substance transportedWater and mineral ionsSucrose, amino acids
Direction of flowUpward only (roots → leaves)Both directions (bidirectional)
Driving forceTranspiration pull (cohesion-tension)Active loading at source (uses ATP)
Cell wallThick, lignifiedThin, not lignified

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

Describe the role of companion cells in translocation.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Companion cells have many mitochondria which carry out aerobic respiration (1 mark)
  • This provides energy (ATP) needed for the active loading of sucrose into sieve tube elements at the source (1 mark)
  • Companion cells maintain the metabolic activity of sieve tube elements, which lack a nucleus (1 mark)
Question 2 [3 marks]

Explain what is meant by "source" and "sink" in translocation, giving one example of each.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • A source is where sucrose is produced / loaded into the phloem, e.g. a photosynthesising leaf (1 mark)
  • A sink is where sucrose is used or stored / unloaded from the phloem (1 mark)
  • Example of sink: root, growing shoot tip, fruit, storage organ such as a potato tuber (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Describe and explain the results of the ringing experiment as evidence that sugars are transported in the phloem.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • When a ring of bark (containing phloem) is removed, tissue above the ring swells because sugars cannot move downward past the ring (1 mark)
  • Tissue below the ring does not receive sugars and does not swell (1 mark)
  • This shows sugars are transported through the phloem in the bark, not through the xylem in the wood (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

State two differences between xylem and phloem.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Any two from: xylem is dead / phloem is living; xylem transports water / phloem transports sucrose; xylem flows in one direction (upward) / phloem is bidirectional; xylem has lignified walls / phloem does not (1 mark each)
Question 5 [3 marks]

A plant is kept in the dark for several days. Predict what would happen to translocation and explain your answer.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Without light, photosynthesis cannot occur, so no sucrose is produced in the leaves (1 mark)
  • Without sucrose being produced at the source, there is no loading of sucrose into phloem (1 mark)
  • Translocation would slow down / stop, and sinks (growing regions, roots) would be deprived of sucrose for respiration and growth (1 mark)
