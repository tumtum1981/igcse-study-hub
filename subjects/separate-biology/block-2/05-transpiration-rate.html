SB2.5.1

Investigating Rate of Transpiration

Using a Potometer

A potometer measures the rate of water uptake by a cut plant shoot, which is used as an estimate of the rate of transpiration.

Setting Up the Potometer

  1. Cut a leafy shoot underwater (to prevent air entering the xylem)
  2. Assemble the potometer under water, inserting the shoot into the rubber bung while submerged
  3. Lift the potometer out of the water and allow the shoot to equilibrate to the test conditions
  4. Introduce a small air bubble into the capillary tube at the cut end
  5. Measure the distance the air bubble moves along the capillary tube in a set time
  6. Calculate rate of water uptake = distance moved ÷ time
FORMULA

Rate of water uptake = distance moved by air bubble ÷ time
Units: mm/min or cm/min

To calculate volume: Volume = cross-sectional area of capillary × distance moved

Investigating Environmental Factors

The potometer can be used to investigate how different conditions affect transpiration rate:

Factor Investigated How to Change It Expected Effect on Bubble Movement
Wind speed Use a fan at different speeds Higher wind speed → faster bubble movement (faster transpiration)
Temperature Place in warm/cool room or water bath Higher temperature → faster bubble movement
Humidity Place in a bag/chamber with damp tissue for high humidity; or in dry air Higher humidity → slower bubble movement
Light intensity Use lamps at different distances or in dark room Higher light → faster bubble movement (stomata open more)

Control variables for each investigation: all other environmental factors, the same plant shoot (or same species/size), same length of time for each reading.

Limitations of the Potometer

The potometer does not directly measure transpiration — it measures water uptake. There are important differences:

  • Some water taken up is used for photosynthesis (not lost by transpiration)
  • Some water is retained in cells to maintain turgor
  • Therefore, the potometer gives an overestimate of transpiration rate
  • The cut shoot is not in its natural state, which may affect results
  • If air enters the xylem, the bubble movement does not reflect water uptake accurately

Despite these limitations, the potometer is useful for comparing relative rates of transpiration under different conditions.

Calculating and Interpreting Results

Example results table:

Condition Distance moved in 5 min (mm) Rate (mm/min)
Still air, 20°C153.0
Fan (wind), 20°C357.0
Still air, 30°C285.6
Still air, 20°C, high humidity61.2

Interpretation: wind and temperature increase the rate of transpiration; high humidity decreases it. These results match the expected patterns from theory.

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

Describe how you would set up a potometer to investigate the effect of wind speed on transpiration rate.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Cut a leafy shoot underwater and assemble the potometer under water (to prevent air entering xylem) (1 mark)
  • Allow to equilibrate, introduce an air bubble, and measure how far it moves per minute at each fan speed (1 mark)
  • Keep all other variables constant (temperature, humidity, light, same shoot) and repeat measurements to calculate a mean (1 mark)
Question 2 [2 marks]

In a potometer experiment, the air bubble moves 24 mm in 4 minutes. The capillary tube has a cross-sectional area of 0.5 mm². Calculate the volume of water taken up per minute.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Rate of movement = 24 ÷ 4 = 6 mm/min (1 mark)
  • Volume = 6 × 0.5 = 3 mm³/min (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Explain why a potometer overestimates the rate of transpiration.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • The potometer measures water uptake, not water loss by transpiration (1 mark)
  • Some of the water taken up is used for photosynthesis (1 mark)
  • Some water is used to maintain cell turgor — not all water taken up is lost through stomata (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

Explain why the leafy shoot must be cut underwater when setting up a potometer.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • If the shoot is cut in air, an air bubble may enter the xylem vessels (1 mark)
  • An air bubble would break the water column in the xylem, preventing accurate measurement of water uptake (1 mark)
Question 5 [4 marks]

A student uses a potometer on a sunny day and again on a cloudy, humid day with the same plant. Predict the results and explain the differences.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • On the sunny day, the air bubble would move faster (higher rate of water uptake) (1 mark)
  • Higher light intensity on the sunny day means stomata are more open, allowing more water vapour to escape (1 mark)
  • On the cloudy/humid day, the concentration gradient between the leaf and air is reduced (1 mark)
  • Less diffusion of water vapour out through stomata → lower transpiration rate → slower bubble movement (1 mark)
