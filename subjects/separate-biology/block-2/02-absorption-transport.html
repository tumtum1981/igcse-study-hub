Absorption and Transport of Nutrients

The Small Intestine as a Site of Absorption

After digestion, food molecules are small enough to be absorbed through the wall of the small intestine into the bloodstream. The small intestine is specifically adapted to maximise absorption.

Key adaptations of the small intestine:

  • Very long: approximately 6–7 metres, providing a large surface area
  • Highly folded inner wall: further increases the surface area
  • Villi: finger-like projections that greatly increase the surface area
  • Microvilli: tiny projections on the surface of each villus cell (brush border) for even more surface area
  • Rich blood supply: maintains a concentration gradient for absorption
  • Thin walls: short diffusion distance for molecules to cross

Structure of a Villus

Structure Description Function in Absorption
Epithelial cells Single layer of cells on the surface of the villus Very thin - short diffusion distance for nutrient molecules
Microvilli (brush border) Tiny finger-like projections on epithelial cells Further increase surface area for absorption
Blood capillaries Network of tiny blood vessels inside the villus Absorb glucose and amino acids; carry them to the liver via the hepatic portal vein
Lacteal Central lymph vessel inside the villus Absorbs fatty acids and glycerol (fats); carries them into the lymphatic system

How Different Nutrients are Absorbed

Glucose and Amino Acids

  • Glucose (from carbohydrate digestion) and amino acids (from protein digestion) are water-soluble
  • They are absorbed by active transport into the epithelial cells of the villi
  • From the epithelial cells, they move into the blood capillaries by diffusion
  • The blood carries them along the hepatic portal vein directly to the liver
  • The liver regulates blood glucose levels and processes amino acids

Fats (Lipids)

  • Fats are digested into fatty acids and glycerol in the small intestine
  • Bile salts emulsify fats into tiny droplets (micelles) to aid digestion and absorption
  • Fatty acids and glycerol are fat-soluble and diffuse directly through the cell membrane of epithelial cells
  • Inside the epithelial cells, they are reassembled into triglycerides (fats)
  • The fats are packaged into chylomicrons and enter the lacteal (lymph vessel)
  • Lymph carries the fats through the lymphatic system and eventually drains into the blood at the thoracic duct

Water

  • Water is absorbed mainly in the large intestine (colon)
  • It moves by osmosis from the lumen (where solute concentration is lower after digestion) into the blood

Transport to the Liver and Around the Body

Nutrient Route After Absorption Mechanism
Glucose Blood capillary → hepatic portal vein → liver → general circulation Active transport into capillaries; diffusion within blood
Amino acids Blood capillary → hepatic portal vein → liver → general circulation Active transport into capillaries; diffusion within blood
Fats (as chylomicrons) Lacteal → lymph vessels → thoracic duct → blood near heart Diffusion into lacteal; lymph flow
Water Large intestine wall → blood capillaries Osmosis

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

Describe three structural adaptations of the small intestine that increase the efficiency of absorption.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Any three from: very long length / folded inner wall / villi (finger-like projections) / microvilli (brush border) on epithelial cells / rich blood supply (capillary network) / thin epithelial walls (short diffusion distance) (1 mark each)
Question 2 [3 marks]

Describe how glucose is absorbed from the small intestine and transported to the liver.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Glucose is absorbed by active transport into the epithelial cells of the villi (1 mark)
  • Glucose passes into the blood capillaries in the villi (1 mark)
  • Blood travels along the hepatic portal vein to the liver (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Describe how fats are absorbed in the small intestine and how they enter the bloodstream.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Fatty acids and glycerol diffuse through the epithelial cell membranes into the villus cells (1 mark)
  • They are reassembled into fats (triglycerides/chylomicrons) and enter the lacteal (lymph vessel) (1 mark)
  • Lymph carries fats through the lymphatic system to the thoracic duct, which drains into the blood (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

State the name of the blood vessel that carries absorbed glucose and amino acids directly to the liver, and explain why going to the liver first is important.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Hepatic portal vein (1 mark)
  • The liver regulates blood glucose levels and can process or store excess glucose / amino acids (1 mark)
Question 5 [2 marks]

Explain why the absorption of glucose requires active transport rather than simple diffusion.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Active transport moves glucose against its concentration gradient (from lower to higher concentration) (1 mark)
  • This requires energy (from ATP/respiration); simple diffusion only moves molecules down a concentration gradient (1 mark)
