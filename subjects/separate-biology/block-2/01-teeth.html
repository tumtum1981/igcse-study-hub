Mechanical Digestion - Teeth

Digestion and the Alimentary Canal

Digestion is the mechanical and chemical breakdown of large, insoluble food molecules into small, water-soluble molecules.

The alimentary canal is a tube running through the body. Food is digested in the alimentary canal. The soluble products are absorbed and the indigestible residues are expelled (egested).

The process of mechanical digestion mainly occurs in the mouth by means of the teeth, through a process called mastication (chewing).

Types of Human Teeth

Humans are omnivores (organisms that eat both animal and plant material). We have four types of teeth:

Tooth Type Location Function
Incisors Front of the mouth Sharp-edged, chisel-shaped; used for biting off pieces of food
Canines Either side of the incisors More pointed; used for gripping and tearing food
Premolars Behind the canines Larger teeth; used for chewing and grinding food
Molars Back of the mouth Large teeth; used for chewing food (includes wisdom teeth)

Two Sets of Teeth

Mammals have two sets of teeth during their lifetime:

  • Milk teeth (deciduous teeth): Start growing at about 5 months old. By 24-30 months, most children have a set of 20 teeth.
  • Permanent teeth: The first set begins to fall out at about age 7. Twenty replacement teeth plus 12 new teeth make a complete set of 32 permanent teeth. Most people have all their permanent teeth by about 17 years of age.

Structure of a Tooth

Part Description
Crown The visible part of the tooth above the gum
Root The part embedded in the gum and jawbone
Enamel Covers the crown; the hardest substance made by animals. Can be dissolved by acids
Dentine Under the enamel; hard but not as hard as enamel. Contains channels with living cytoplasm
Pulp cavity Centre of the tooth; contains nerves and blood vessels that supply the dentine
Cement Covers the root; has fibres that attach the tooth to the jawbone while allowing slight movement
Gum Tissue that overlays the jaws and surrounds the base of the teeth

Dental Decay and Gum Disease

Both tooth decay and gum disease are caused by bacteria in the mouth.

How Plaque Forms

  • Bacteria and substances from saliva form a sticky film called plaque on teeth
  • Plaque builds up especially next to gums and between teeth
  • If not removed, plaque hardens to form tartar, which cannot be removed by brushing

Dental Decay (Cavities)

  • Bacteria on the tooth surface feed on sugars deposited on the teeth
  • The bacteria respire the sugars, producing acid
  • The acid dissolves the calcium salts in the tooth enamel
  • Enamel is dissolved in patches, exposing the dentine
  • Dentine is softer and dissolves more quickly, forming cavities
  • If untreated, bacteria reach the pulp cavity and cause a painful abscess

Gum Disease

  • Bacteria in plaque can infect the gums, causing swelling and inflammation
  • If bacteria spread down around the root, the tooth becomes loose and may need removing

Preventing Tooth Decay

  • Diet: Avoid eating sugar at frequent intervals (sweets, fizzy drinks, squash)
  • Brushing: Regular brushing helps prevent gum disease. Fluoride toothpaste reduces bacteria and increases resistance to decay
  • Dental check-ups: Visit the dentist every 6 months so any decay or gum disease can be treated early
