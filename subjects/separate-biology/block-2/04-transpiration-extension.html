SB2.4.1

Transpiration Extension

Water Potential

Water potential is a measure of the tendency of water molecules to move from one place to another. It determines the direction of osmosis.

  • Pure water has the highest water potential (defined as 0)
  • Solutions have a lower (more negative) water potential than pure water — the more dissolved substances, the lower the water potential
  • Water moves by osmosis from a region of higher (less negative) water potential to a region of lower (more negative) water potential

In the transpiration stream, a water potential gradient exists from the soil (relatively high) → root cells → xylem → mesophyll cells → air spaces in leaf (low) → atmosphere (lowest). This gradient drives the movement of water through the plant.

Turgor and Wilting

Water relations are critical for maintaining plant structure:

State Description Cause
Turgid Cell is firm; vacuole is full of water; cell wall is stretched but exerts inward pressure (wall pressure) Water enters by osmosis when surrounding solution has higher water potential than the cell
Flaccid Cell has lost water; vacuole is smaller; cell is soft; no wall pressure Water leaves by osmosis when surrounding solution has lower water potential than the cell
Plasmolysed Cytoplasm shrinks away from the cell wall; in extreme water loss Very high external solute concentration draws water out rapidly; only occurs in plant cells

Wilting occurs when the rate of water loss by transpiration exceeds the rate of water uptake by the roots. Cells become flaccid, and the plant loses structural support. Wilting is reversed when water is available and cells become turgid again.

Stomata Control Mechanism

Guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata through changes in turgidity:

Stomata Open (during the day, when light is available)

  • Light triggers photosynthesis in guard cells → sugars produced → water potential in guard cells falls
  • Water enters guard cells by osmosis from surrounding cells
  • Guard cells become turgid
  • The inner wall of guard cells is thicker — uneven expansion causes the cell to bow outward
  • The stoma opens

Stomata Close (at night, or when water is scarce)

  • No light → no photosynthesis → sugars used up → water potential in guard cells rises
  • Water leaves guard cells by osmosis
  • Guard cells become flaccid
  • The stoma closes
  • In drought conditions, the hormone abscisic acid (ABA) is produced, which causes stomata to close to prevent water loss

Adaptations to Reduce Transpiration

Plants in dry or hot environments have evolved structural and behavioural adaptations:

Adaptation How it reduces transpiration
Thick waxy cuticleImpermeable layer on leaf surface reduces evaporation directly through the epidermis
Fewer stomataFewer openings for water vapour to escape through
Sunken stomataStomata in pits trap a layer of humid air, reducing the concentration gradient between leaf and air
Hairy leavesHairs trap a layer of moist air near the leaf surface, reducing the concentration gradient
Small leaf areaLess surface area exposed to evaporation and sunlight
Stomata open only at night (CAM plants)Reduces water loss; CO₂ stored as organic acids overnight and used during day when stomata closed

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

Explain what is meant by water potential and the direction in which water moves by osmosis.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Water potential is a measure of the tendency of water to move from one area to another (1 mark)
  • Pure water has the highest water potential (0); solutions have lower (more negative) water potential (1 mark)
  • Water moves by osmosis from higher to lower water potential (from less concentrated to more concentrated solution) (1 mark)
Question 2 [3 marks]

Explain the mechanism by which stomata open in the light.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Light causes photosynthesis in guard cells, producing sugars, which lowers the water potential of the guard cells (1 mark)
  • Water enters the guard cells by osmosis, making them turgid (1 mark)
  • The uneven thickening of guard cell walls causes them to bow outward, opening the stoma (1 mark)
Question 3 [2 marks]

Define wilting and explain what causes it.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Wilting is when a plant droops/loses structural rigidity because its cells become flaccid (1 mark)
  • It occurs when the rate of water loss by transpiration exceeds the rate of water uptake by the roots, so cells lose water and become flaccid (1 mark)
Question 4 [3 marks]

Describe three adaptations of a xerophyte that reduce water loss by transpiration, explaining how each works.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Thick waxy cuticle — impermeable layer reduces direct evaporation through the epidermis (1 mark)
  • Sunken stomata — trapped humid air in pits reduces the concentration gradient between leaf and air, slowing diffusion of water vapour (1 mark)
  • Small/reduced leaf area — less surface area for evaporation; or hairy leaves trap moist air near the surface (1 mark)
Question 5 [3 marks]

A plant cell is placed in a concentrated sugar solution. Describe what happens to the cell, using the terms water potential, osmosis, and plasmolysis.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • The concentrated sugar solution has a lower water potential than the cell contents (1 mark)
  • Water leaves the cell by osmosis, moving down the water potential gradient (1 mark)
  • The cell loses water, becomes flaccid, and may plasmolyse — the cytoplasm shrinks away from the cell wall as more water is lost (1 mark)
