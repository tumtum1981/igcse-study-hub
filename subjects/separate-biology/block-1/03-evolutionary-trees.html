Evolutionary Trees

Summary

Evolutionary trees (also called phylogenetic trees) are diagrams that show how different species are related to each other through evolution. They illustrate common ancestors and how species have diverged over time. Branches on the tree represent the evolutionary relationships between organisms, with closely related species sharing more recent common ancestors.

Key Definitions

Term Definition
Phylogenetic tree A diagram showing the evolutionary relationships between organisms
Common ancestor An ancestral species from which two or more different species have evolved
Evolution The gradual change in organisms over many generations
Node A branching point on a tree, representing a common ancestor
Divergence When one species splits into two or more separate species

Reading Evolutionary Trees

Understanding how to read evolutionary trees is an essential skill:

Feature What It Represents
Root (base) The oldest common ancestor of all organisms on the tree
Branches Lines of evolutionary descent from ancestors to descendants
Nodes (branching points) Points where species diverged; represent common ancestors
Tips (ends of branches) Living species or groups (or extinct species at dead ends)
Branch length May represent time or amount of genetic change (depends on tree type)

Interpreting Relationships

  • Closely related species share a more recent common ancestor (their branches join closer to the tips)
  • Distantly related species share an older common ancestor (their branches join closer to the root)
  • Species that share more features are usually grouped closer together on the tree
  • The order of branches can be rotated around nodes without changing the meaning

Evidence Used to Build Evolutionary Trees

Scientists use various types of evidence to determine evolutionary relationships:

Type of Evidence Description
Physical features (morphology) Comparing body structures, skeletal features, and anatomy
DNA sequences Comparing genetic code - more similar DNA = more closely related
Protein sequences Comparing amino acid sequences in proteins like haemoglobin
Fossil record Examining preserved remains of ancient organisms
Embryo development Comparing how embryos develop in different species

Example: Primate Evolutionary Tree

Consider a simplified evolutionary tree of primates:

  • Humans and chimpanzees share the most recent common ancestor (most closely related)
  • Gorillas branched off earlier, so they are slightly more distantly related
  • Orangutans diverged even earlier
  • Monkeys share an older common ancestor with all apes

DNA evidence supports this: humans and chimpanzees share about 98-99% of their DNA.

Key Points to Remember

  • Evolutionary trees show how species are related through common ancestors
  • Nodes represent common ancestors where species diverged
  • Species closer together on the tree are more closely related
  • DNA and protein comparisons provide strong evidence for relationships
  • Trees can be redrawn in different ways while showing the same relationships
