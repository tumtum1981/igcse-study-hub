Evolutionary Trees and Gene Sequencing

Summary

Evolutionary trees are diagrams that show how different species are related to each other through evolution. They illustrate common ancestors and how species have diverged over time. By comparing gene sequences (DNA) between species, scientists can determine how closely related they are and build more accurate evolutionary trees.

Key Definitions

Term Definition
Evolutionary tree A diagram showing the evolutionary relationships between organisms
Common ancestor An ancestral species from which two or more different species have evolved
Evolution The gradual change in organisms over many generations
Gene sequence The order of bases (A, G, C, T) in a section of DNA

Classification and Evolutionary Relationships

The Linnaean classification system provides information about evolutionary relationships. Two species within the same genus probably share a recent common ancestor. As you descend through each level, the number of species in each group decreases:

Reading Evolutionary Trees

Evolutionary trees show how species evolved over time from a common ancestor:

  • Closely related species share a more recent common ancestor (their branches join closer to the present day)
  • Distantly related species share an older common ancestor (their branches join further back in time)
  • The order of branches can be rotated around branching points without changing the meaning

Example: Hominidae Family

The five members of the Hominidae family (chimpanzee, bonobo, human, gorilla, orangutan) can be shown on an evolutionary tree spanning back 13 million years:

  • Chimpanzees and bonobos share the most recent common ancestor (most closely related)
  • Humans share a common ancestor with chimpanzees and bonobos
  • Gorillas branched off earlier
  • Orangutans diverged the earliest (about 13 million years ago)

Gene Sequencing

Gene sequences change very slowly over time. By comparing similarities and differences between the gene sequences of different species, we can determine which ones are the most closely related.

Comparing DNA Sequences

This table shows a DNA sequence from one small section of one gene from several mammals:

Organism DNA Sequence
Baleen whale A G A G C C C T T G A A A A T
Toothed whale A G A G C C C T T G A A A A T
Cow T G A G C G C C T G A A A A T
Hippo A G A C C G C C T G A A A A T
Tapir A A C A C C C T C A A C A A C
Zebra A A C A C C C T C A A C A A C

What the DNA Tells Us

  • Baleen whale and toothed whale have identical sequences - they are the most closely related pair
  • Tapir and zebra also have identical sequences - they are closely related to each other
  • The hippo and cow have only 3 differences from the whales - they are fairly closely related
  • The tapir and zebra have 7 differences from the whales - they are more distantly related
Species Order DNA Differences from Whales
Hippo Artiodactyla (even-toed) 3
Cow Artiodactyla (even-toed) 3
Tapir Perissodactyla (odd-toed) 7
Zebra Perissodactyla (odd-toed) 7

This DNA evidence shows that whales are most closely related to the hippo and cow (Artiodactyla - even-toed hoofed mammals). This has helped scientists reclassify organisms that they didn't previously know were related.

Key Points to Remember

  • Evolutionary trees show how species are related through common ancestors
  • Species closer together on the tree share a more recent common ancestor
  • Gene sequences change slowly over time - more similar DNA = more closely related
  • DNA comparison can reveal unexpected relationships (e.g. whales and hippos)
  • DNA evidence has helped reclassify organisms into more accurate evolutionary trees
