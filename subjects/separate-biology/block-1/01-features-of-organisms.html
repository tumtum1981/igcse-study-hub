SB1.1.1

Features of Organisms

Summary

Living organisms are classified into groups based on their shared features and characteristics. Classification helps scientists study, identify, and understand the relationships between different organisms. The main classification system divides all life into five kingdoms: Animals, Plants, Fungi, Protoctists, and Prokaryotes.

Key Definitions

Term Definition
Classification The process of sorting organisms into groups based on shared features
Kingdom The largest group in classification, containing organisms with similar basic features
Taxonomy The science of naming and classifying organisms
Organism Any living thing that displays the characteristics of life

The Five Kingdoms

All living organisms are classified into five kingdoms based on their cell structure, nutrition, and other key features:

Kingdom Key Features Examples
Animals Multicellular, no cell wall, heterotrophic (feed on other organisms), can move Humans, fish, insects, birds
Plants Multicellular, cellulose cell wall, autotrophic (make own food by photosynthesis), contain chloroplasts Trees, grasses, ferns, mosses
Fungi Mostly multicellular, chitin cell wall, saprophytic nutrition (feed on dead matter), no chloroplasts Mushrooms, yeast, moulds
Protoctists Mostly unicellular, eukaryotic (have a nucleus), varied nutrition methods Amoeba, Paramecium, algae
Prokaryotes Unicellular, no true nucleus (DNA free in cytoplasm), cell wall (not cellulose) Bacteria, cyanobacteria

Features Used for Classification

Scientists use various observable and structural features to classify organisms:

  • Cell type: Prokaryotic (no nucleus) or eukaryotic (has nucleus)
  • Cell structure: Presence of cell wall, type of cell wall material
  • Number of cells: Unicellular or multicellular
  • Nutrition: Autotrophic (makes own food) or heterotrophic (feeds on others)
  • Body structure: Presence of tissues, organs, body symmetry
  • Reproduction: Sexual or asexual, type of reproductive structures

Why Classification is Important

  • Helps scientists communicate about organisms using standard names
  • Shows evolutionary relationships between organisms
  • Helps identify new species and understand biodiversity
  • Organises biological knowledge in a logical way
  • Assists in conservation efforts by understanding species relationships

Key Points to Remember

  • Classification groups organisms by shared features
  • There are five kingdoms: Animals, Plants, Fungi, Protoctists, and Prokaryotes
  • Prokaryotes lack a true nucleus; all other kingdoms are eukaryotic
  • Plants are autotrophic; animals and fungi are heterotrophic
  • Features used include cell type, nutrition method, and body structure

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

State three features that distinguish organisms in the Plant kingdom from organisms in the Animal kingdom.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Plants have a cellulose cell wall; animals do not have a cell wall (1 mark)
  • Plants are autotrophic/make their own food by photosynthesis; animals are heterotrophic/feed on other organisms (1 mark)
  • Plants have chloroplasts; animals do not have chloroplasts (1 mark)
Question 2 [2 marks]

Explain the difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Prokaryotic cells do not have a true nucleus/membrane-bound nucleus (1 mark)
  • Eukaryotic cells have a true nucleus/DNA enclosed in a nuclear membrane (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Describe three features of organisms in the Fungi kingdom.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Have cell walls made of chitin (1 mark)
  • Are saprophytic/feed on dead organic matter (1 mark)
  • Do not have chloroplasts/cannot photosynthesise (1 mark)
  • Are mostly multicellular (accept: some are unicellular e.g., yeast) (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

Suggest two reasons why scientists classify organisms into groups.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • To identify and name organisms using a universal system (1 mark)
  • To show evolutionary relationships between organisms (1 mark)
  • To organise biological knowledge (1 mark)
  • To help with conservation efforts (1 mark) (any two)
