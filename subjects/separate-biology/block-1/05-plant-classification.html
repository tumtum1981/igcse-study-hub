SB1.5.1

Plant Classification

Summary

The Plant kingdom is divided into groups based on their structural features, particularly how they reproduce and transport water. The main division is between flowering plants (angiosperms) and non-flowering plants. Flowering plants are further divided into monocotyledons (monocots) and dicotyledons (dicots) based on the structure of their seeds, leaves, and flowers.

Key Definitions

Term Definition
Angiosperm A flowering plant that produces seeds enclosed within a fruit
Cotyledon A seed leaf; the first leaf/leaves to emerge from a germinating seed
Monocotyledon A flowering plant with one cotyledon (seed leaf)
Dicotyledon A flowering plant with two cotyledons (seed leaves)
Vascular tissue Tissue for transporting water (xylem) and sugars (phloem) in plants

Major Plant Groups

Plants can be classified into several major groups based on their features:

Group Key Features Examples
Mosses (Bryophytes) No true roots, stems or leaves; no vascular tissue; reproduce by spores; need water for reproduction Sphagnum moss, liverworts
Ferns (Pteridophytes) Have roots, stems and leaves; have vascular tissue; reproduce by spores on underside of leaves Bracken, maidenhair fern
Conifers (Gymnosperms) Have vascular tissue; produce seeds in cones; needle-like leaves; no flowers or fruits Pine, spruce, fir trees
Flowering plants (Angiosperms) Have vascular tissue; produce flowers, fruits and seeds; most diverse plant group Roses, grasses, oak trees

Flowering vs Non-Flowering Plants

Feature Flowering Plants Non-Flowering Plants
Flowers Present - for reproduction Absent
Seeds Enclosed in fruit Exposed in cones or absent (spores instead)
Reproduction Seeds produced after pollination Spores or naked seeds
Examples Apple tree, tulip, wheat Fern, moss, pine tree

Monocots vs Dicots

Flowering plants are divided into two main groups:

Feature Monocotyledons (Monocots) Dicotyledons (Dicots)
Seed leaves (cotyledons) One Two
Leaf veins Parallel (run side by side) Branching/net-like pattern
Flower parts In multiples of 3 (3, 6, 9...) In multiples of 4 or 5
Vascular bundles in stem Scattered throughout stem Arranged in a ring
Root system Fibrous roots (many thin roots) Taproot (one main root)
Examples Grass, wheat, rice, lilies, orchids Roses, beans, oak trees, sunflowers

Identifying Monocots and Dicots

You can identify whether a flowering plant is a monocot or dicot by examining:

  • Leaves: Look at the vein pattern - parallel veins = monocot, branching veins = dicot
  • Flowers: Count the petals - multiples of 3 = monocot, multiples of 4 or 5 = dicot
  • Seeds: If you can see the seed germinating, count the seed leaves
  • Stems: Cut a cross-section - scattered vascular bundles = monocot, ring arrangement = dicot

Key Points to Remember

  • Plants are classified by their reproductive structures and vascular tissue
  • Flowering plants (angiosperms) produce seeds enclosed in fruits
  • Non-flowering plants reproduce by spores (mosses, ferns) or naked seeds (conifers)
  • Monocots have one seed leaf, parallel leaf veins, and flower parts in 3s
  • Dicots have two seed leaves, branching leaf veins, and flower parts in 4s or 5s

Question 3 [3 marks]

Describe how ferns differ from flowering plants in their method of reproduction.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Ferns reproduce by spores; flowering plants reproduce by seeds (1 mark)
  • Ferns do not produce flowers; flowering plants do (1 mark)
  • Ferns do not produce fruits; flowering plants produce fruits containing seeds (1 mark)
  • Fern spores are produced on the underside of leaves (1 mark) (any three)
Question 4 [2 marks]

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER

  • Mosses need water for reproduction/male gametes swim to female gametes (1 mark)
