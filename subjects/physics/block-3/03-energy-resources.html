P3.3.1

Energy Resources

Summary

This page covers Sessions 3 and 4: Energy Resources & Renewable Energy

Energy resources are sources of energy that can be used to generate electricity or provide power. They are classified as renewable (will not run out) or non-renewable (will eventually run out).

UK Electricity Generation: About 30% of UK electricity comes from wind power, and nearly 5% from solar power. The UK is increasingly moving towards renewable sources.

Non-Renewable Energy Resources

Non-renewable resources are finite - they will run out and cannot be replaced in a human lifetime.

Resource How it works Advantages Disadvantages
Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) Burned to heat water, steam turns turbines Reliable, high energy output, cheap Produces CO2 (climate change), pollution, will run out
Nuclear Nuclear fission heats water, steam turns turbines No CO2 emissions, reliable, high output Radioactive waste, risk of accidents, expensive to build

Renewable Energy Resources

Renewable resources will not run out and are constantly replenished.

Resource How it works Advantages Disadvantages
Solar Solar cells convert sunlight to electricity Free energy, no pollution, low running costs Only works in daylight, expensive panels, weather dependent
Wind Wind turns turbine blades connected to generator Free energy, no pollution Unreliable (no wind = no power), visual pollution, noise
Hydroelectric Falling water turns turbines Reliable, no pollution, can respond quickly to demand Requires flooding valleys, damages habitats, expensive to build
Tidal Moving tides turn turbines Predictable, reliable twice daily Expensive, affects marine life, limited locations
Wave Wave motion drives generators Free energy, no pollution Unreliable, can be damaged by storms, expensive
Geothermal Heat from underground rocks heats water Reliable, free energy, no fuel needed Only available in certain locations, expensive to set up
Biomass Burning plant/animal material or biogas Carbon neutral (if replanted), uses waste Requires land, produces some pollution

How Renewable Resources Generate Electricity

Wind Power:

  • Wind turbines have an electrical generator in the top (nacelle)
  • Wind spins the turbine blades, which turn the generator to produce electricity
  • No fuel costs or emissions, but output varies with wind speed and weather
  • Can be onshore or offshore (offshore wind farms are common in the UK)

Solar Power:

  • Solar panels (photovoltaic cells) convert radiation from the Sun directly into electricity
  • No emissions during operation
  • Output varies depending on location, weather conditions, and time of year
  • A sunny day in autumn generates more than a cloudy day in winter

Hydroelectric Power:

  • A dam creates a high reservoir of water
  • Water falls through turbines connected to electrical generators
  • Can be turned on quickly when electricity demand increases (just open a valve)
  • Very reliable, but building dams destroys habitats by flooding valleys

Tidal Power:

  • A barrage (barrier) is built across a river estuary
  • Water fills up at high tide, then is released through turbines at low tide
  • Very reliable because there are two high tides every day
  • Can block shipping access and damage estuary habitats

Wave Power:

  • Wave generators use the up-and-down movement of waves to drive generators
  • No emissions, but can be damaged by storms and output is unreliable

Geothermal Power:

  • Uses heat from hot rocks underground to heat water into steam
  • Steam spins electrical generators
  • Only available in certain locations (near volcanic activity)

Biofuels:

  • Power stations burn crops or plant material to generate electricity
  • Does produce pollution when burned
  • Almost carbon neutral because the next crops absorb CO2 as they grow
  • Uses land that could otherwise grow food

Comparing Energy Resources

Factor Non-renewable Renewable
Availability Will run out Will not run out
Reliability Very reliable Often weather-dependent
Environmental impact CO2 and pollution Generally cleaner
Power output High and constant Often lower or variable
Cost Lower running costs Higher setup costs

Environmental Impacts

Fossil fuels cause:

  • Carbon dioxide emissions → climate change
  • Sulfur dioxide emissions → acid rain
  • Particulates → respiratory problems
  • Oil spills → water pollution

Renewable resources can cause:

  • Visual pollution (wind turbines, solar farms)
  • Habitat destruction (hydroelectric dams)
  • Noise pollution (wind turbines)
  • Effects on wildlife (tidal barrages)

Key Points to Remember

  • Non-renewable: fossil fuels and nuclear
  • Renewable resources will not run out
  • Fossil fuels produce CO2 and contribute to climate change
  • Renewable resources often have variable output
  • Each resource has advantages and disadvantages

Question 1 [2 marks]

Define the term 'renewable energy resource'.

  • An energy resource that will not run out (1 mark)

Question 3 [2 marks]

Explain why wind power is described as unreliable.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Wind speed varies/is not constant (1 mark)
  • If there is no wind, no electricity is generated (1 mark)
Question 4 [3 marks]

Compare the environmental impacts of a coal-fired power station with a wind farm.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Coal produces CO2/greenhouse gases, wind does not (1 mark)
  • Coal contributes to climate change/acid rain (1 mark)
  • Wind causes visual/noise pollution but no air pollution (1 mark)
Question 5 [2 marks]

Explain why hydroelectric power stations can respond quickly when electricity demand increases.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Water is already stored in the reservoir at height (1 mark)
  • Only need to open a valve to allow water to fall through the turbines (1 mark)
Question 6 [2 marks]

Which renewable energy resource produces pollution when used? Explain why it is still considered almost carbon neutral.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Biofuels produce pollution when burned (1 mark)
  • Almost carbon neutral because the next crops absorb CO2 as they grow, balancing out emissions (1 mark)
Question 7 [3 marks]

Describe three energy transfers that take place when energy from the Sun causes electrical energy to be generated by a wind turbine.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER

Any three from:

  • Infrared/electromagnetic waves from the Sun heat the atmosphere (1 mark)
  • Thermal energy transfers to kinetic energy of wind (1 mark)
  • Kinetic energy of wind transfers to kinetic energy of turbine blades (1 mark)
  • Kinetic energy of turbine transfers to kinetic energy of generator (1 mark)
  • Generator transfers kinetic energy to electrical energy (1 mark)
