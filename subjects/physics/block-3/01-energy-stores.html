Energy Stores

Summary

Energy is the ability to do work. Energy is measured in joules (J). Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between different energy stores. This is the law of conservation of energy.

The Eight Energy Stores

Energy Store Description Example
Kinetic Energy of moving objects A moving car, running person
Gravitational potential Energy due to height above ground A ball held above the ground
Elastic potential Energy stored in stretched/compressed objects Stretched spring, bent bow
Chemical Energy stored in chemical bonds Food, fuels, batteries
Thermal Energy due to temperature (internal energy) Hot water, warm radiator
Magnetic Energy stored in magnetic fields Fridge magnets, MRI machines
Electrostatic Energy stored in electric fields Thunderclouds, charged balloon
Nuclear Energy stored in atomic nuclei Uranium, the Sun

Energy Transfer Pathways

Energy moves between stores through four main pathways:

Pathway Description Example
Mechanical (by forces) When a force moves an object Pushing a box, lifting weights
Electrical When charge flows through a circuit Current in a wire
Heating When thermal energy moves from hot to cold Hand warming on a radiator
Radiation Energy transferred by waves Light, sound, microwaves

Conservation of Energy

The law of conservation of energy states:

LAW

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one store to another.

This means the total energy in a closed system always stays the same. Energy can change form but the total amount is constant.

Energy Transfer Examples

Dropping a ball:

  • Ball held high: gravitational potential store
  • Ball falling: gravitational potential → kinetic store
  • Ball hits ground: kinetic → thermal store (and sound)

Firing a catapult:

  • Pulled back: elastic potential store
  • Released: elastic potential → kinetic store
  • Stone rises: kinetic → gravitational potential store

Turning on a light bulb:

  • Battery/mains: chemical/electrical energy
  • Electrical pathway transfers energy to bulb
  • Bulb: thermal store + radiation (light)

Wasted Energy

In most energy transfers, some energy is dissipated (spread out) to the surroundings as thermal energy. This is often called "wasted" energy because it is not useful.

  • Friction transfers kinetic energy to thermal energy
  • Electrical resistance transfers electrical energy to thermal energy
  • The energy is not lost - it just becomes less useful

Key Points to Remember

  • Energy is measured in joules (J)
  • There are 8 energy stores
  • Energy is transferred through 4 pathways
  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed
  • Wasted energy is usually thermal energy dissipated to surroundings
