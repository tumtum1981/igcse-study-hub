Conservation of Energy Calculations

Summary

This page covers Session 5: Energy Summary & Conservation Calculations

The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between stores. When an object changes height or speed (assuming no energy is dissipated), the change in gravitational potential energy equals the change in kinetic energy.

This principle lets us calculate the speed of a falling object from its height, or the height reached by a moving object, without knowing anything about the forces involved.

Key Formulas

Formula Meaning Units
ΔGPE = m × g × Δh Gravitational potential energy J = kg × N/kg × m
KE = ½ × m × v² Kinetic energy J = kg × (m/s)²
ΔGPE = KE Conservation of energy (no dissipation) m × g × Δh = ½ × m × v²

Key assumption: In these calculations we assume no energy is dissipated (e.g. no air resistance or friction), so all energy transfers between the gravitational and kinetic stores.

Type 1: Finding Kinetic Energy from Height

When an object falls from a height, it loses gravitational potential energy and gains kinetic energy. If no energy is dissipated, the KE gained equals the GPE lost.

Worked Example: Roller Coaster

Question: A roller coaster has a mass of 850 kg. How much energy will it have in the kinetic store after falling 40 m from rest?

Solution:

Change in GPE = change in KE

ΔGPE = m × g × Δh

ΔGPE = 850 × 10 × 40

ΔGPE = 340 000 J (340 kJ) in the kinetic store

Worked Example: Skydiver

Question: A skydiver has a mass of 68 kg. How much energy will she have in the kinetic store after falling 50 m from rest?

Solution:

ΔGPE = m × g × Δh

ΔGPE = 68 × 10 × 50

ΔGPE = 34 000 J (34 kJ) in the kinetic store

Type 2: Finding Height from Speed

When an object is launched upward, it converts kinetic energy into gravitational potential energy. At the highest point, all KE has been converted to GPE.

Worked Example: Ball Kicked Upward

Question: A ball of mass 0.5 kg is kicked up into the air with a speed of 8 m/s. How high will it reach?

Solution:

Step 1: Find the kinetic energy

KE = ½ × m × v² = ½ × 0.5 × 8² = 16 J

Step 2: At the highest point, all KE has become GPE

ΔGPE = m × g × Δh

16 = 0.5 × 10 × Δh

Δh = 16 ÷ 5 = 3.2 m

Worked Example: Stunt Double

Question: A stunt double (mass 74 kg) is shot vertically out of a cannon at a speed of 22 m/s. What height will they reach?

Solution:

KE = ½ × 74 × 22² = ½ × 74 × 484 = 17 908 J

ΔGPE = m × g × Δh

17 908 = 74 × 10 × Δh

Δh = 17 908 ÷ 740 = 24 m (2 s.f.)

Type 3: Finding Speed from Height

We can also rearrange to find the speed of an object after falling from a height, or the launch speed needed to reach a certain height.

Worked Example: Ball Speed

Question: At what speed is a 0.5 kg ball kicked up at, if it reaches a height of 10 m? (g = 9.8 N/kg)

Solution:

Step 1: Find the GPE at the top

ΔGPE = 0.5 × 9.8 × 10 = 49 J

Step 2: Set equal to KE and solve for v

49 = ½ × 0.5 × v²

v² = 49 ÷ 0.25 = 196

v = √196 = 14 m/s

Worked Example: Volleyball

Question: A volleyball of mass 280 g is hit up into the air and reaches a height of 3.80 m. How fast was it moving the moment after it was hit?

Solution:

Step 1: Convert mass: 280 g = 0.280 kg

ΔGPE = 0.280 × 10 × 3.80 = 10.64 J

Step 2: Set equal to KE

10.64 = ½ × 0.280 × v²

v² = 10.64 ÷ 0.140 = 76

v = √76 = 8.7 m/s (2 s.f.)

Energy Transfer Chains

Conservation of energy also applies to energy transfer chains. If no energy is dissipated, the total energy stays the same throughout each transfer.

Worked Example: Catapult and Ball

Question: A ball of mass 0.2 kg is shot up into the air with a catapult. It reaches a height of 15.0 m (g = 10 N/kg). Assume no energy dissipates.

1) GPE at the top:

GPE = 0.2 × 10 × 15.0 = 30 J

2) KE as it left the catapult: = 30 J (all energy was in the kinetic store)

3) Elastic energy before release: = 30 J (all energy was in the elastic store)

4) KE when it hits the ground: = 30 J (GPE converts back to KE)

The full energy transfer chain:

  • Elastic potential store (stretched catapult) → 30 J
  • Kinetic store (ball launched upward) → 30 J
  • Gravitational potential store (ball at highest point) → 30 J
  • Kinetic store (ball falling back down) → 30 J

The energy is always 30 J — it just transfers between different stores.

Efficiency Review

Not all energy transfers are 100% efficient. When calculating efficiency:

Efficiency = useful output energy ÷ total input energy

  • Efficiency has no unit because it is a ratio of two energies (joules ÷ joules)
  • It can be expressed as a decimal (e.g. 0.30) or a percentage (e.g. 30%)
  • Efficiency is always less than or equal to 1 (or 100%)

Key Points to Remember

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred between stores
  • When no energy is dissipated: ΔGPE = ΔKE
  • To find KE from height: calculate GPE using m × g × Δh
  • To find height from speed: calculate KE using ½ × m × v², then rearrange ΔGPE = m × g × Δh
  • To find speed from height: calculate GPE, set equal to ½ × m × v², solve for v
  • Always convert units first (e.g. grams to kg, kJ to J)
  • In energy chain problems, the total energy stays the same at every stage

  • ΔGPE = m × g × Δh (1 mark)

Question 3 [4 marks]

A toy rocket of mass 1.2 kg is shot up into the air with a speed of 45 m/s. Calculate the maximum height it will reach. Assume no energy is dissipated. (g = 10 N/kg)

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • KE = ½ × m × v² = ½ × 1.2 × 45² (1 mark)
  • KE = 1215 J (1 mark)
  • 1215 = 1.2 × 10 × Δh (1 mark)
  • Δh = 1215 ÷ 12 = 101.25 = 100 m (2 s.f.) (1 mark)
Question 4 [4 marks]

A toy rocket of mass 1.8 kg reaches a height of 150 m. Calculate the speed at which it was launched. (g = 9.8 N/kg)

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • ΔGPE = m × g × Δh = 1.8 × 9.8 × 150 = 2646 J (1 mark)
  • KE = ½ × m × v² (1 mark)
  • 2646 = ½ × 1.8 × v², so v² = 2940 (1 mark)
  • v = √2940 = 54 m/s (2 s.f.) (1 mark)
Question 5 [2 marks]

Explain why efficiency has no unit.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Efficiency is a ratio of useful output energy to total input energy (1 mark)
  • Both are measured in joules, so the units cancel out (1 mark)
