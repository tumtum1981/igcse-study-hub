Explaining Convection

What is Convection?

Convection is the transfer of thermal energy through a fluid (liquid or gas) by the movement of the fluid itself. It cannot occur in solids because particles in a solid cannot flow.

How Convection Works

  1. A fluid near a heat source is heated → particles gain kinetic energy and move faster
  2. The heated fluid expands → the same mass now occupies a greater volume
  3. The density of the heated fluid decreases (same mass, greater volume → lower density)
  4. The less dense, warmer fluid rises (it is less dense than the surrounding cooler fluid)
  5. Cooler, denser fluid from above sinks to take its place near the heat source
  6. This sets up a convection current — a continuous circulation of the fluid

Why Convection Cannot Occur in Solids

In a solid, particles are held in fixed positions by strong forces. They can only vibrate — they cannot flow or move from place to place. Therefore the bulk movement required for convection cannot happen. Solids transfer thermal energy only by conduction.

Examples of Convection Currents

Example Explanation
Heating a room with a radiator Air near the radiator warms, expands, becomes less dense and rises. Cool air near the ceiling sinks and flows toward the radiator. A convection current circulates warm air around the room.
Sea breeze During the day, land heats up faster than the sea. Air above the land warms, rises, and cool air from the sea flows in to replace it, creating a sea breeze.
Refrigerator Cooling element at the top cools air, which becomes denser and sinks. Warmer air at the bottom rises to be cooled, creating a convection current that cools the contents evenly.
Boiling water in a kettle Water at the bottom is heated, becomes less dense and rises. Cooler water sinks to replace it, creating convection currents that distribute heat through the water.

Comparing Methods of Thermal Energy Transfer

Method Medium needed How energy is transferred
Conduction Solids (and liquids/gases to a lesser extent) Particle vibrations and collisions; free electrons in metals
Convection Fluids (liquids and gases) only Bulk movement of heated fluid (density differences)
Radiation No medium needed (can travel through vacuum) Infrared electromagnetic waves emitted by all objects above absolute zero

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [4 marks]

Explain how a convection current forms when water in a beaker is heated from below.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Water near the heat source is warmed, gaining kinetic energy (1 mark)
  • It expands, so its density decreases (1 mark)
  • The less dense, warmer water rises; cooler, denser water sinks to replace it (1 mark)
  • This creates a continuous convection current circulating the water (1 mark)
Question 2 [2 marks]

Explain why convection cannot occur in a solid.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • In a solid, particles are held in fixed positions and can only vibrate (1 mark)
  • They cannot flow/move from place to place, so the bulk movement of material required for convection cannot happen (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Explain how a sea breeze forms during a sunny day.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • The land heats up faster than the sea during the day (1 mark)
  • Air above the land warms, expands, becomes less dense and rises (1 mark)
  • Cooler, denser air from over the sea moves in to replace it, creating a breeze blowing from sea to land (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

Suggest why the cooling element in a refrigerator is placed at the top, not the bottom.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Cold air at the top is denser and sinks, displacing warmer air upward (1 mark)
  • This creates a convection current that circulates cold air throughout the refrigerator, cooling it evenly (1 mark)
Question 5 [3 marks]

State the three methods of thermal energy transfer. For each, state whether it can occur in a vacuum.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Conduction — cannot occur in a vacuum (requires particles) (1 mark)
  • Convection — cannot occur in a vacuum (requires fluid particles to move) (1 mark)
  • Radiation (infrared) — CAN occur in a vacuum; it is an electromagnetic wave and does not need a medium (1 mark)
