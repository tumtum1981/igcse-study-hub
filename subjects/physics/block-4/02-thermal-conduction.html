Thermal Conduction and Insulation

What is Thermal Conduction?

Thermal conduction is the transfer of thermal energy (heat) through a material without the material itself moving. It occurs in solids, and to a lesser extent in liquids and gases.

How Conduction Works

When one end of a solid is heated:

  1. Particles at the hot end vibrate more (they have more kinetic energy)
  2. These particles collide with neighbouring particles, transferring some of their kinetic energy
  3. Energy passes along the material from the hot end to the cold end, particle by particle
  4. Net transfer of energy is always from high temperature to low temperature (down the temperature gradient)

Conductors and Insulators

Materials vary greatly in how well they conduct thermal energy:

Type Examples Why
Good conductors Metals (copper, aluminium, iron) Metals have free electrons that move through the structure. These electrons carry kinetic energy rapidly from hot to cold regions — much faster than vibration alone
Poor conductors (insulators) Wood, plastic, rubber, glass, air, wool No free electrons; energy only transfers by particle vibration, which is much slower. Trapped air pockets in materials like wool are especially poor conductors

This is why metals feel cold to the touch even at room temperature — they conduct heat away from your skin quickly, making them feel cold. Wood at the same temperature feels warmer because it conducts heat away more slowly.

Factors Affecting Rate of Conduction

The rate of conduction through a material depends on:

  • Temperature difference — larger temperature difference → faster rate of conduction
  • Thickness of material — thicker material → slower conduction (longer path)
  • Cross-sectional area — larger area → faster conduction (more particles in contact)
  • Material type — metals conduct faster than non-metals due to free electrons

Insulation in Practice

Thermal insulation reduces the rate of energy transfer by conduction (and other methods). Common examples:

  • Loft insulation — fibreglass or mineral wool traps air between fibres; air is a very poor conductor
  • Cavity wall insulation — foam or fibres fill the gap between walls, reducing conduction and convection
  • Double glazing — air (or argon gas) gap between two panes reduces conduction through the window
  • Vacuum flask (thermos) — vacuum between walls eliminates conduction and convection (vacuum has no particles to conduct heat)
  • Clothing — fabrics trap air next to the skin; air is a poor conductor, reducing heat loss

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

Describe, in terms of particles, how thermal energy is conducted along a metal rod when one end is heated.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Particles at the hot end gain kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously (1 mark)
  • They collide with and transfer energy to neighbouring particles (1 mark)
  • In metals, free electrons also transfer energy rapidly through the metal / energy passes from hot end to cold end (1 mark)
Question 2 [2 marks]

Explain why metals are better thermal conductors than non-metals.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Metals have free electrons that can move through the structure (1 mark)
  • These free electrons carry kinetic energy much more rapidly than vibration alone, making metals much better conductors (1 mark)
Question 3 [2 marks]

Explain why a metal spoon and a wooden spoon at the same room temperature feel different when touched.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • The metal spoon conducts heat away from your hand quickly, so it feels cold (1 mark)
  • The wooden spoon is a poor conductor, so it does not transfer heat from your hand as quickly, making it feel warmer (1 mark)
Question 4 [3 marks]

Explain how loft insulation, cavity wall insulation, and double glazing all reduce heat loss by conduction.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Loft insulation: fibrous material traps pockets of air; air is a poor conductor so energy transfer is slowed (1 mark)
  • Cavity wall insulation: foam/fibres fill the air gap between walls, reducing conduction (and convection) across the gap (1 mark)
  • Double glazing: the air (or argon) gap between the two panes is a poor conductor, reducing energy transfer through the window (1 mark)
Question 5 [3 marks]

A vacuum flask has a vacuum between its inner and outer walls. Explain why this is effective at keeping drinks hot.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • A vacuum contains no particles (1 mark)
  • Conduction requires particles to vibrate and collide, so it cannot occur through a vacuum (1 mark)
  • Convection also cannot occur in a vacuum (no fluid to move), so energy transfer by both methods is eliminated, keeping the drink hot for longer (1 mark)
