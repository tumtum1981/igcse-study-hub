Newton's Second Law (F=ma)

Summary

Newton's Second Law describes the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration. It states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the resultant force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This is one of the most important equations in physics.

Newton's Second Law Formula

F = m x a
Force = mass x acceleration

This can be rearranged to find mass or acceleration:

a = F / m
Acceleration = Force / mass
m = F / a
Mass = Force / acceleration

Where:

  • F = resultant force (measured in Newtons, N)
  • m = mass (measured in kilograms, kg)
  • a = acceleration (measured in m/s2)

Understanding the Equation

Relationship Explanation
F is proportional to a If force doubles (with constant mass), acceleration doubles
a is inversely proportional to m If mass doubles (with constant force), acceleration halves
F = 0 No resultant force means no acceleration (constant velocity or stationary)

The Newton Unit

The unit of force is defined by Newton's Second Law:

1 N = 1 kg x 1 m/s2
One Newton is the force needed to accelerate 1 kg by 1 m/s2

Newton's Three Laws of Motion

Law Statement
First Law An object remains at rest or moves at constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant force
Second Law F = ma - acceleration is proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass
Third Law For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction

Practical Applications

  • Cars: More powerful engines provide greater force, causing greater acceleration
  • Rockets: As fuel burns and mass decreases, acceleration increases for the same thrust
  • Sports: A tennis ball accelerates more than a bowling ball with the same force
  • Braking: Heavier vehicles need more braking force to decelerate at the same rate

Key Points to Remember

  • F = ma (Force = mass x acceleration)
  • Force is measured in Newtons (N)
  • The force in the equation is the resultant force
  • Greater force = greater acceleration (for same mass)
  • Greater mass = smaller acceleration (for same force)
  • No resultant force = no acceleration

Question 1 [2 marks]

Calculate the force needed to accelerate a 1500 kg car at 2 m/s2.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • F = m x a (1 mark)
  • F = 1500 x 2 = 3000 N (1 mark)
Question 2 [3 marks]

A resultant force of 600 N acts on an object, causing it to accelerate at 4 m/s2. Calculate: (a) the mass of the object, (b) the new acceleration if the force is increased to 900 N.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • (a) m = F / a = 600 / 4 = 150 kg (1 mark)
  • (b) a = F / m (1 mark)
  • a = 900 / 150 = 6 m/s2 (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

A car of mass 1200 kg accelerates from rest to 15 m/s in 6 seconds. Calculate: (a) the acceleration, (b) the resultant force acting on the car.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • (a) a = (v - u) / t = (15 - 0) / 6 = 2.5 m/s2 (1 mark)
  • (b) F = m x a (1 mark)
  • F = 1200 x 2.5 = 3000 N (1 mark)
Question 4 [4 marks]

A lorry of mass 8000 kg is travelling at 20 m/s. The brakes apply a constant braking force of 16000 N. Calculate: (a) the deceleration, (b) the time taken to stop, (c) the braking distance.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • (a) a = F / m = 16000 / 8000 = 2 m/s2 deceleration (1 mark)
  • (b) t = (v - u) / a = (0 - 20) / -2 = 10 s (1 mark)
  • (c) Using area under v-t graph: distance = 1/2 x 10 x 20 (1 mark)
  • Distance = 100 m (1 mark)
