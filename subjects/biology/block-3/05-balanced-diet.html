B3.5.1

Balanced Diet

Summary

A balanced diet contains the right proportions of all the food groups needed to keep both your mind and body healthy. The seven main food groups are carbohydrates, proteins, lipids (fats), vitamins, minerals, fibre, and water. Nutritional requirements vary depending on age, activity level, and lifestyle.

The Main Food Groups

Our bodies need a variety of different nutrients to stay healthy. Nutrients are essential for the maintenance of life and for growth. The main food groups are:

Food Group Found In Function
Carbohydrates Bread, rice, pasta, potatoes Broken down into sugars; provide energy for the body (e.g. muscle contractions)
Proteins Beans, meat, eggs, fish Broken down into amino acids; essential for growth and repair of cells and tissues
Lipids (fats) Butter, oil, avocado, nuts Broken down into glycerol and fatty acids; provide energy, insulation, and make up cell membranes
Vitamins Fruits, vegetables, fish, eggs Keep cells healthy, support wound healing, maintain bones and teeth
Minerals Meat, dairy, leafy vegetables Making red blood cells (iron), maintaining strong bones and teeth (calcium)
Fibre Fruit, vegetables, whole grains Moves food through the gut; reduces risk of bowel cancer and type II diabetes
Water Drinks, food with high water content Essential for most chemical reactions in the body; makes up about 60% of our bodies
IMPORTANT

Ideally, we should get carbohydrates from whole foods (bread, rice, potatoes) and not from processed foods with added sugars (sweets, fizzy drinks).

Vitamins

Vitamins are needed in small amounts but are essential for good health.

Vitamin D

  • Found in oily fish (e.g. sardines) and egg yolks
  • Also produced in our skin when exposed to UV light
  • Essential for strong bones and teeth, and a healthy immune system

Vitamin C

  • Found in many fruits and vegetables (e.g. oranges, broccoli)
  • Essential to keep our cells healthy and support wound healing

Minerals

Like vitamins, minerals are needed in small amounts for specific functions.

Iron

  • Found in red meat and green leafy vegetables (e.g. kale)
  • Essential for making red blood cells that carry oxygen around the body

Calcium

  • Found in dairy products (e.g. cheese) and green leafy vegetables (e.g. cabbage)
  • Helps to maintain strong bones and teeth

Fibre and Water

Fibre

Fibre is not a nutrient but is still an essential part of a healthy diet.

  • Fibre cannot be broken down or absorbed by our body
  • It helps to move food through our gut and can reduce the risk of bowel cancer
  • It adds bulk to food and helps us to feel fuller for longer
  • It also reduces the risk of type II diabetes

Water

Around 60% of our bodies are made up of water. It is essential for most of the chemical reactions in our bodies.

  • The NHS recommends we drink enough water so our urine is a pale, yellow colour
  • This replaces water lost in our breath, sweat, and urine

A Balanced Diet

A balanced diet helps to keep both your mind and body healthy. The key principles are:

  • Try to eat fruit and vegetables with every meal
  • Eat a rainbow of different coloured foods each day to ensure variety
  • The World Health Organization recommends getting less than 10% of energy from sugar and less than 30% from fats

Saturated vs Unsaturated Fats

Not all fats are equal. Unsaturated fats are preferable to saturated fats.

Unsaturated Fats (healthier) Saturated Fats (less healthy)
Avocado, salmon, nuts Meat, cake, butter
Associated with better heart health Associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease

The Eatwell Guide

The UK government produced the Eatwell Guide to show the proportions of food groups we should eat. It recommends:

  • Eating plenty of fruit and vegetables (at least 5 portions a day)
  • Basing meals on starchy carbohydrates (choosing wholegrain where possible)
  • Including some protein sources (beans, pulses, fish, eggs, meat)
  • Choosing lower fat and lower sugar dairy options
  • Using unsaturated oils in small amounts
  • Drinking 6-8 glasses of water a day
  • Eating foods high in fat, salt, and sugar less often and in small amounts

Varying Nutritional Needs

At different times of your life, you will need a different balance of nutrients. What is balanced for one person may not be for another.

Factor Effect on Diet
Children / teenagers More protein (for growth), more carbohydrates and fats (more active)
Pregnant women More protein (growing baby needs new cells)
Active people More carbohydrates and fats (more energy needed for exercise)
Less active adults Less protein (not growing), less carbohydrate and fat (less energy needed)

Key Points to Remember

  • The seven food groups are: carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, vitamins, minerals, fibre, and water
  • Carbohydrates provide energy; proteins are for growth and repair; lipids provide energy, insulation, and make cell membranes
  • Vitamin D is for bones and teeth; Vitamin C keeps cells healthy
  • Iron makes red blood cells; calcium maintains bones and teeth
  • Fibre is not a nutrient but helps move food through the gut
  • Unsaturated fats are healthier than saturated fats
  • Nutritional needs vary with age, activity level, and lifestyle
