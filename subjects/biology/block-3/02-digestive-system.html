B3.2.1

The Digestive System

Summary

The digestive system breaks down large food molecules into smaller, soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the blood. This process involves both mechanical digestion (physical breakdown) and chemical digestion (using enzymes).

Why Digestion is Needed

Food contains large, insoluble molecules that cannot pass through the gut wall into the blood. Digestion breaks these down into small, soluble molecules that can be:

  • Absorbed through the gut wall into the blood
  • Transported around the body to cells
  • Used for energy, growth, and repair

Parts of the Digestive System

Organ Function
Mouth Mechanical digestion (teeth), chemical digestion (salivary amylase breaks down starch)
Oesophagus Transports food from mouth to stomach by peristalsis
Stomach Produces hydrochloric acid and protease enzymes; churns food
Small intestine Main site of digestion and absorption; receives enzymes from pancreas
Large intestine Absorbs water from undigested food
Rectum Stores faeces before egestion
Anus Where faeces leave the body (egestion)

Associated Organs

Organ Function
Salivary glands Produce saliva containing amylase enzyme
Liver Produces bile (stored in gall bladder); bile emulsifies fats
Gall bladder Stores bile and releases it into the small intestine
Pancreas Produces digestive enzymes (amylase, protease, lipase) released into small intestine

Types of Digestion

Mechanical digestion:

  • Teeth break food into smaller pieces
  • Increases surface area for enzymes to work on
  • Churning in the stomach mixes food with enzymes

Chemical digestion:

  • Enzymes break down large molecules into smaller ones
  • Starch is broken down into glucose
  • Proteins are broken down into amino acids
  • Lipids are broken down into fatty acids and glycerol

What Happens to Each Nutrient

Nutrient Enzyme Product
Starch Amylase Maltose then glucose
Protein Protease Amino acids
Lipids Lipase Fatty acids + glycerol

The Role of Bile

Bile is produced by the liver and stored in the gall bladder. It has two important functions:

  • Emulsifies fats: Breaks large fat droplets into smaller droplets, increasing surface area for lipase
  • Neutralises stomach acid: Bile is alkaline, creating the right pH for enzymes in the small intestine

Key Points to Remember

  • Digestion breaks down large insoluble molecules into small soluble ones
  • Mechanical digestion increases surface area; chemical digestion uses enzymes
  • The small intestine is the main site of digestion and absorption
  • Bile emulsifies fats and neutralises stomach acid
  • Each food type has a specific enzyme that breaks it down
