B3.4.1

Enzymes Continued

Summary

This topic continues our study of enzymes, focusing on enzyme specificity and the factors that affect enzyme activity. Each enzyme can only catalyse one specific reaction because its active site has a unique shape that only fits one substrate.

Enzyme Specificity

Enzymes are specific for their substrate. This means each enzyme can only catalyse one particular chemical reaction.

The Lock and Key Model

We can use a lock and key as a model to explain enzyme specificity:

  • The lock represents the enzyme
  • The keyhole represents the active site
  • The key represents the substrate

Just like only one key has the correct shape to fit a particular lock's keyhole, only one substrate has the correct shape to fit an enzyme's active site.

Naming Enzymes

Most enzymes have names ending in -ase. The name usually tells you what substrate the enzyme breaks down:

Enzyme Group Substrate Products
Carbohydrases Carbohydrates (starch) Sugars (glucose, maltose)
Proteases Proteins Amino acids
Lipases Lipids (fats) Fatty acids + glycerol

Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

Temperature

  • Increasing temperature initially increases the rate of reaction
  • Higher temperatures give particles more kinetic energy, causing more collisions between enzyme and substrate
  • Each enzyme has an optimum temperature where it works best (usually around 37°C for human enzymes)
  • If the temperature is too high, the enzyme will denature

pH

  • Each enzyme has an optimum pH where it works best
  • Extreme pH values (too acidic or too alkaline) cause the enzyme to denature
  • Different enzymes work best at different pH values depending on where they function in the body

Denaturation

When an enzyme is denatured:

  • The bonds holding the enzyme's shape break
  • The active site changes shape permanently
  • The substrate can no longer fit into the active site
  • The enzyme can no longer catalyse the reaction
IMPORTANT

Enzymes are denatured - NOT killed or dead!

Enzymes are not living things, they are proteins. They lose their function when denatured.

Metabolism

Enzymes control all the chemical reactions in the body. The sum of all these reactions is called metabolism.

  • Metabolic reactions include breaking down food molecules (digestion)
  • They also include building up larger molecules from smaller ones
  • Without enzymes, these reactions would be too slow to sustain life

Key Points to Remember

  • Enzymes are specific - each enzyme only catalyses one reaction
  • The lock and key model explains enzyme specificity
  • Enzyme names often end in -ase (carbohydrase, protease, lipase)
  • Temperature and pH affect enzyme activity
  • Enzymes have optimum conditions where they work best
  • Enzymes denature (not die) at extreme temperatures or pH
