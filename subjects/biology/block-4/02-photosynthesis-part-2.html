Photosynthesis Part 2 - Limiting Factors

Chloroplasts and Chlorophyll

Photosynthesis takes place in the chloroplasts found inside plant cells. Chloroplasts contain a green pigment called chlorophyll.

  • Chlorophyll absorbs light energy from the sun
  • This light energy is used to drive the chemical reactions of photosynthesis
  • Chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light but reflects green light (which is why plants appear green)
  • Cells near the top of a leaf have more chloroplasts to absorb more light
SYMBOL EQUATION

6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2

The symbol equation shows that 6 molecules of carbon dioxide and 6 molecules of water react to produce 1 molecule of glucose and 6 molecules of oxygen.

Factors that Affect the Rate of Photosynthesis

The rate of photosynthesis can be increased or decreased by changing the conditions around a plant. A limiting factor is any factor that, when in short supply, reduces the rate of photosynthesis below its maximum possible rate.

The three main limiting factors are:

  • Light intensity
  • Carbon dioxide concentration
  • Temperature

Effect of Light Intensity

Light provides the energy needed for photosynthesis. As light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases - up to a point.

  • In low light: light is the limiting factor - increasing light intensity increases the rate
  • At high light: another factor (usually CO2 concentration or temperature) becomes limiting
  • Further increases in light intensity have no effect once the plateau is reached

Inverse square law: Light intensity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the light source. Moving a lamp twice as far away reduces the light intensity to one quarter.

FORMULA

Light intensity ∝ 1 / distance²

Effect of Carbon Dioxide Concentration

Carbon dioxide is one of the reactants of photosynthesis. If its concentration is increased, the rate of photosynthesis increases - until another factor becomes limiting.

  • In normal air, CO2 is about 0.04% (400 ppm) - often a limiting factor
  • Increasing CO2 concentration in greenhouses can significantly boost crop yields
  • At very high CO2 levels, light or temperature becomes the limiting factor

Effect of Temperature

Photosynthesis involves enzymes. Temperature affects the rate of enzyme-catalysed reactions.

Temperature Effect on Rate of Photosynthesis Reason
Too low (e.g. 5°C) Very slow rate Enzyme molecules and substrates have low kinetic energy; few successful collisions
Optimum (~25–35°C) Maximum rate Enzymes work at peak efficiency
Too high (e.g. 45°C+) Rate drops to zero Enzymes are denatured - their active sites change shape permanently

Investigating the Rate of Photosynthesis

The rate of photosynthesis can be measured by counting the number of oxygen bubbles produced per minute by an aquatic plant such as Elodea (Canadian pondweed).

Method

  • Place the plant in water with a known distance from a lamp
  • Count the number of oxygen bubbles produced per minute
  • Change one variable at a time (e.g. distance from lamp, CO2 concentration, temperature)
  • Keep all other variables constant (controlled variables)

Using Algal Balls

Another method uses algae (e.g. Chlorella) trapped in sodium alginate balls. These are placed in hydrogen carbonate indicator solution:

  • Yellow: CO2 has increased (acidic) - more respiration than photosynthesis
  • Orange/red: normal CO2 level (no net change)
  • Purple/magenta: CO2 has decreased (alkaline) - more photosynthesis than respiration

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [2 marks]

State what is meant by a "limiting factor" in the context of photosynthesis.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • A limiting factor is any factor that, when in short supply, slows down or limits the rate of photosynthesis (1 mark)
  • If the limiting factor is increased, the rate of photosynthesis will increase (1 mark)
Question 2 [3 marks]

Write the balanced symbol equation for photosynthesis and name the reactants and products.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • 6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2 (1 mark)
  • Reactants: carbon dioxide and water (1 mark)
  • Products: glucose and oxygen (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Explain why increasing temperature beyond the optimum decreases the rate of photosynthesis.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Photosynthesis is controlled by enzymes (1 mark)
  • At temperatures above the optimum, enzymes are denatured (1 mark)
  • The active site changes shape so the substrate cannot bind / the enzyme-substrate complex cannot form (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

A student measures the rate of photosynthesis of Elodea at different distances from a lamp. Suggest why increasing the distance from 10 cm to 20 cm reduces the rate more than increasing from 30 cm to 40 cm.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Light intensity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance (1 mark)
  • So at shorter distances, a small change in distance causes a greater change in light intensity than at longer distances (1 mark)
Question 5 [3 marks]

Describe how algal balls and hydrogen carbonate indicator can be used to investigate the rate of photosynthesis. Include what colour changes you would expect to observe.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Algal balls are placed in hydrogen carbonate indicator solution under different light conditions (1 mark)
  • In bright light, photosynthesis exceeds respiration, CO2 is removed, solution turns purple/magenta (1 mark)
  • In darkness, only respiration occurs, CO2 increases, solution turns yellow (1 mark)
