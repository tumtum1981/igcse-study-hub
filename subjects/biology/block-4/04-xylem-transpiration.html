Xylem and Transpiration

Structure of Xylem Vessels

Xylem is the tissue that transports water and dissolved minerals from the roots to the rest of the plant. Xylem vessels are highly adapted for this function.

  • Dead cells — xylem vessels are formed from dead cells; there is no cytoplasm blocking the flow of water
  • Hollow tubes — no end walls between cells, forming a continuous open tube for water to flow through
  • Lignified walls — walls strengthened with lignin, which is waterproof and provides structural support to prevent the vessel from collapsing
  • Pits in walls — small gaps (pits) allow water to move sideways from vessel to vessel and into surrounding cells

Transpiration

Transpiration is the evaporation of water from the leaves of a plant. It occurs mainly through the stomata on the lower surface of leaves.

The Transpiration Stream

Water moves through the plant in a continuous stream driven by transpiration:

  1. Roots: water enters root hair cells by osmosis (soil water has a higher water potential than the cytoplasm of root cells)
  2. Stem: water moves up xylem vessels. As water evaporates from leaves, it creates a tension (negative pressure) that pulls water up from below — this is called the cohesion-tension mechanism
  3. Leaves: water moves from xylem into mesophyll cells, then evaporates into air spaces and diffuses out through stomata as water vapour
KEY CONCEPT

Cohesion: water molecules stick to each other (hydrogen bonds)
Tension: evaporation from leaves pulls the water column upward
Together these maintain a continuous column of water from roots to leaves

Stage Process Mechanism
Water uptake Enters root hair cells from soil Osmosis (water potential gradient)
Water movement up stem Moves up xylem vessels Cohesion-tension (transpiration pull)
Water exits leaf Evaporates from mesophyll cells, diffuses through stomata Evaporation and diffusion

Adaptations of Root Hair Cells

Root hair cells are adapted for absorbing water and minerals from the soil:

  • Long, thin extensions (root hairs) — greatly increase the surface area for absorption
  • No cell wall blocking diffusion — large surface area in direct contact with soil water
  • High solute concentration in cytoplasm — lower water potential than soil water, so water enters by osmosis
  • Many mitochondria — provide energy (ATP) for active transport of mineral ions

Importance of Transpiration

  • Cooling — evaporation of water from leaves cools the plant (like sweating in animals)
  • Water supply — drives the transpiration stream, delivering water to all cells for photosynthesis and turgor
  • Mineral transport — dissolved minerals are carried up with water in the transpiration stream
