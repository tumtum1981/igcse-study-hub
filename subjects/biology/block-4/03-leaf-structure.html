Structure of a Leaf

The Leaf as an Organ for Photosynthesis

The leaf is the main organ for photosynthesis in most plants. Its structure is adapted to maximise the absorption of light and the exchange of gases needed for photosynthesis.

Key adaptations of a leaf:

  • Broad and flat - large surface area to absorb light
  • Thin - short diffusion distance for CO2 and O2
  • Contains chlorophyll - absorbs light energy
  • Network of veins - delivers water and minerals, removes glucose
  • Stomata - allow gas exchange (CO2 in, O2 out)

Layers of a Leaf

Layer Description Function
Upper epidermis Single layer of transparent cells, no chloroplasts; covered by a waxy cuticle Protects the leaf; the cuticle reduces water loss; transparent to allow light through
Palisade mesophyll Column-shaped cells packed tightly near the top of the leaf; contain many chloroplasts Main site of photosynthesis; palisade cells positioned near the top to absorb the most light
Spongy mesophyll Irregularly shaped cells with large air spaces between them; fewer chloroplasts than palisade cells Air spaces allow CO2 and O2 to diffuse through the leaf; some photosynthesis occurs here
Lower epidermis Single cell layer containing stomata and guard cells Gas exchange (CO2 enters, O2 exits); controls water vapour loss
Veins (vascular bundles) Contain xylem and phloem tissue running through the leaf Xylem delivers water; phloem transports glucose away from the leaf

Stomata and Gas Exchange

Stomata (singular: stoma) are tiny pores found mainly on the lower surface of leaves. Each stoma is surrounded by two guard cells.

  • When guard cells are turgid (full of water), the stoma opens - allowing gas exchange
  • When guard cells are flaccid (lose water), the stoma closes - reducing water loss
  • Stomata open during the day (when photosynthesis needs CO2) and close at night
  • More stomata on the lower surface reduces water loss from direct sunlight on the upper surface

Gases that diffuse through stomata:

  • CO2 diffuses IN - reactant for photosynthesis (moves down its concentration gradient from air into leaf)
  • O2 diffuses OUT - product of photosynthesis (moves down its gradient from leaf into air)
  • Water vapour diffuses OUT - this is called transpiration

Adaptations of Palisade Cells

Palisade cells are the primary photosynthetic cells of the leaf. They are highly adapted:

  • Column shape - allows many cells to be packed side by side in a thin layer, maximising light absorption
  • Many chloroplasts - up to 70 per cell, can move within the cell to face the light
  • Located near the top - closest to the light source, so absorb maximum light before it is absorbed by other layers
  • Thin cell walls - short diffusion distance for CO2 to reach the chloroplasts

The Role of Xylem and Phloem

Tissue Function in the Leaf Substance Transported
Xylem Brings water and dissolved minerals from the roots up to the leaf Water (used as a reactant in photosynthesis and to keep cells turgid)
Phloem Carries the glucose (as sucrose) produced by photosynthesis away from the leaf to the rest of the plant Sucrose (dissolved glucose for transport)

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

Describe three ways in which the structure of a leaf is adapted for photosynthesis.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Any three from: broad/flat shape provides large surface area for light absorption / thin leaf means short diffusion distance for CO₂ / palisade cells near top contain many chloroplasts / stomata allow CO₂ to diffuse in / air spaces in spongy mesophyll allow gases to circulate / veins deliver water needed as a reactant (1 mark each)
Question 2 [3 marks]

Explain why palisade mesophyll cells are well adapted for photosynthesis.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Palisade cells contain many chloroplasts which absorb light energy (1 mark)
  • They are positioned near the top of the leaf, closest to the light source (1 mark)
  • Their column shape allows many cells to be packed together, maximising light absorption in a thin layer (1 mark)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Explain how stomata open and close, and why they are mainly found on the lower surface of the leaf.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Guard cells become turgid when they absorb water → stoma opens; guard cells become flaccid when they lose water → stoma closes (1 mark)
  • This controls the balance between gas exchange and water loss (1 mark)
  • Being on the lower surface reduces water loss because the upper surface would be directly exposed to sunlight and lose more water by transpiration (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

State the roles of xylem and phloem in a leaf.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Xylem transports water (and minerals) to the leaf from the roots (1 mark)
  • Phloem transports glucose (as sucrose) away from the leaf to other parts of the plant (1 mark)
Question 5 [3 marks]

A student notices that when a plant wilts (loses water), its stomata close. Explain the advantage of this response to the plant.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • When the plant loses water, guard cells become flaccid and stomata close (1 mark)
  • This reduces further water loss by transpiration through the stomata (1 mark)
  • This prevents the plant from losing more water and wilting further / helps conserve water (1 mark)
