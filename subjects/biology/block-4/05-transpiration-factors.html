Factors that Affect Transpiration

Four Key Factors

The rate of transpiration — the loss of water vapour from leaves — is affected by environmental conditions. Four main factors determine how quickly water vapour diffuses through the stomata.

Factor Effect on Transpiration Rate Explanation
Temperature Higher temperature → increases rate Water molecules have more kinetic energy, evaporate faster from mesophyll cells; steeper concentration gradient between leaf and air
Light intensity Higher light intensity → increases rate Light causes stomata to open (guard cells become turgid); more open stomata = more water vapour can escape
Humidity Higher humidity → decreases rate More water vapour already in the air reduces the concentration gradient between the leaf and the air; less diffusion occurs
Wind speed Higher wind speed → increases rate Wind removes water vapour from around the leaf, maintaining a steep concentration gradient; increases diffusion rate

Measuring Transpiration — The Potometer

A potometer is used to measure the rate of water uptake by a plant shoot, which is an estimate of the rate of transpiration.

How to use a potometer:

  1. Set up the potometer under water to avoid air entering the xylem
  2. Allow the plant to equilibrate to conditions, then introduce a small air bubble at the cut end of the capillary
  3. Measure how far the air bubble moves in a set time — this gives the rate of water uptake in cm/min or cm³/min
  4. Change one environmental variable (e.g. wind, temperature, humidity) and repeat
FORMULA

Rate of water uptake = distance moved by bubble ÷ time taken
Units: cm/min or mm/min

Xerophytes — Plants Adapted to Dry Conditions

Xerophytes are plants that live in dry (arid) habitats and have special adaptations to reduce water loss by transpiration:

  • Thick waxy cuticle — reduces evaporation from the leaf surface
  • Sunken stomata — stomata in pits trap moist air, reducing the concentration gradient
  • Reduced leaf area — cacti have spines instead of leaves, greatly reducing surface area for evaporation
  • Rolled leaves — some grasses roll their leaves in dry conditions, trapping humid air inside
  • Stomata open at night — some xerophytes (e.g. cacti) only open stomata at night when it is cooler, reducing water loss

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [4 marks]

Explain how each of the following affects the rate of transpiration: (a) increased temperature, (b) increased humidity.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • (a) Higher temperature gives water molecules more kinetic energy (1 mark), so evaporation from mesophyll cells is faster, increasing the concentration gradient between leaf and air, so water vapour diffuses out faster (1 mark)
  • (b) Higher humidity means more water vapour in the air already (1 mark), so the concentration gradient between the leaf and the air is reduced, slowing diffusion of water vapour out through stomata (1 mark)
Question 2 [2 marks]

A potometer is used to measure water uptake in a plant. The air bubble moves 30 mm in 5 minutes. Calculate the rate of water uptake.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Rate = distance ÷ time = 30 ÷ 5 = 6 mm/min (1 mark for working, 1 mark for correct answer with units)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Explain why increasing wind speed increases the rate of transpiration.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Wind blows water vapour away from the leaf surface (1 mark)
  • This maintains a steep concentration gradient between the inside of the leaf (high water vapour) and the air outside (low water vapour) (1 mark)
  • So water vapour continues to diffuse out rapidly through the stomata (1 mark)
Question 4 [3 marks]

Describe three adaptations of a xerophyte that reduce water loss by transpiration.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Any three from: thick waxy cuticle reduces evaporation / sunken stomata trap moist air reducing concentration gradient / reduced leaf area (e.g. spines) reduces surface area for evaporation / rolled leaves trap humid air / opening stomata only at night reduces water loss (1 mark each)
Question 5 [3 marks]

A student uses a potometer to investigate the effect of wind on transpiration rate. State one variable that must be controlled, and explain why the potometer does not directly measure transpiration.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Control variable: temperature / humidity / light intensity (any one) must be kept constant so that only wind speed is changed (1 mark)
  • The potometer measures water uptake not water loss (1 mark)
  • Some water taken up is used for photosynthesis or to keep cells turgid, not lost by transpiration — so the potometer gives an overestimate of transpiration (1 mark)
