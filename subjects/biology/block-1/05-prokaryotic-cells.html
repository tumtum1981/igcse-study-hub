Prokaryotic (Bacterial) Cells

Summary

Prokaryotic cells are simpler and smaller than eukaryotic cells. Bacteria are prokaryotes. The main difference is that prokaryotic cells do not have a true nucleus - their DNA is not enclosed in a membrane.

Structure of a Bacterial Cell

Structure Function
Cell wall Protects and supports the cell (made of peptidoglycan, not cellulose)
Cell membrane Controls what enters and leaves the cell
Cytoplasm Where chemical reactions occur
Chromosomal DNA Single circular chromosome - contains genetic information
Plasmid DNA Small circular rings of extra DNA - can be transferred between bacteria
Ribosomes Smaller than in eukaryotes - site of protein synthesis
Flagellum (some bacteria) Tail-like structure for movement
Capsule (some bacteria) Slimy outer layer for protection

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

Feature Prokaryotic Eukaryotic
Nucleus No true nucleus (DNA free in cytoplasm) True nucleus with membrane
DNA Circular chromosome + plasmids Linear chromosomes in nucleus
Size Smaller (1-5 um) Larger (10-100 um)
Ribosomes Smaller (70S) Larger (80S)
Mitochondria None Present
Examples Bacteria Animals, plants, fungi

Bacterial Reproduction

Bacteria reproduce by binary fission:

  • The circular DNA replicates (copies itself)
  • The cell grows larger
  • The cytoplasm divides
  • Two identical daughter cells are formed

This can happen very quickly - some bacteria divide every 20 minutes!

Key Points to Remember

  • Prokaryotes have no true nucleus
  • DNA is a single circular chromosome
  • Plasmids are extra rings of DNA
  • Bacteria are much smaller than plant/animal cells
  • Bacteria reproduce by binary fission
