B1.2.1

Specialised Cells

Summary

Cells become specialised to carry out particular functions. This process is called differentiation. Specialised cells have specific structures that make them efficient at their job.

Animal Specialised Cells

Red Blood Cells

Adaptation How it helps
No nucleus More room for haemoglobin to carry oxygen
Biconcave disc shape Large surface area for gas exchange
Contains haemoglobin Binds to oxygen to transport it around the body
Flexible Can squeeze through narrow capillaries

Nerve Cells (Neurones)

Adaptation How it helps
Long axon Can transmit impulses over long distances
Branched endings (dendrites) Can connect to many other neurones
Myelin sheath Insulates the axon and speeds up impulse transmission

Sperm Cells

Adaptation How it helps
Tail (flagellum) Allows the sperm to swim to the egg
Many mitochondria Release energy for swimming
Acrosome (head) Contains enzymes to penetrate the egg
Streamlined shape Reduces resistance when swimming

Plant Specialised Cells

Root Hair Cells

Adaptation How it helps
Long hair-like projection Increases surface area for water absorption
Thin cell wall Shortens diffusion distance
No chloroplasts Underground so no light for photosynthesis
Large vacuole Maintains concentration gradient for osmosis

Palisade Mesophyll Cells

Adaptation How it helps
Many chloroplasts Maximum photosynthesis
Located near top of leaf Close to light source
Tall, column shape Many can be packed together
Thin cell walls Easy gas exchange

Key Points to Remember

  • Specialised cells are adapted for specific functions
  • Differentiation is the process of becoming specialised
  • Red blood cells have no nucleus to carry more haemoglobin
  • Root hair cells have projections to increase surface area
  • Structure is always related to function
