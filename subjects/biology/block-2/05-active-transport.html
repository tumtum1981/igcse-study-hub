B2.5.1

Active Transport

Summary

Active transport is the movement of particles against a concentration gradient (from low to high concentration). Unlike diffusion and osmosis, it requires energy from respiration.

Definition

Active Transport
The movement of particles from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration, using energy from respiration

Comparing Transport Methods

Feature Diffusion Osmosis Active Transport
Energy required? No (passive) No (passive) Yes (active)
Direction High to low High to low water potential Low to high
What moves? Any particles Water only Specific particles
Needs membrane? No Yes (partially permeable) Yes (carrier proteins)

Examples of Active Transport

Example What is transported Why active transport is needed
Root hair cells Mineral ions (nitrates, magnesium) Concentration in soil is lower than in root
Small intestine Glucose, amino acids To absorb all nutrients even against gradient
Kidney tubules Glucose, ions To reabsorb useful substances

How Active Transport Works

  • Carrier proteins in the cell membrane bind to specific molecules
  • Energy from ATP (from respiration) changes the protein shape
  • The molecule is released on the other side of the membrane
  • The process requires mitochondria to provide energy

Key Points to Remember

  • Active transport moves particles AGAINST the concentration gradient
  • Energy is required from respiration (ATP)
  • Cells with lots of active transport have many mitochondria
  • Used when diffusion alone cannot provide enough substances
