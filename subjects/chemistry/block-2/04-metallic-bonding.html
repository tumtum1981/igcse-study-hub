Metallic Bonding

Summary

Metallic bonding is the strong electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and a 'sea' of delocalised electrons. This type of bonding explains the unique properties of metals.

Structure of Metals

  • Metal atoms lose their outer electrons
  • This creates a lattice of positive ions
  • The outer electrons become delocalised (free to move)
  • These electrons form a 'sea' around the positive ions
  • The attraction between positive ions and electrons holds the structure together

Properties of Metals Explained

Property Explanation
Good electrical conductors Delocalised electrons can move through the structure carrying charge
Good thermal conductors Delocalised electrons transfer kinetic energy quickly through the metal
High melting/boiling points Strong metallic bonds require lots of energy to break
Malleable (can be bent) Layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking bonds
Ductile (can be drawn into wire) Layers can slide; electrons adjust to new positions
Shiny appearance Delocalised electrons reflect light

Alloys

An alloy is a mixture of two or more metals (or a metal with a non-metal like carbon).

  • Different sized atoms disrupt the regular layers
  • This makes it harder for layers to slide
  • Alloys are usually harder and stronger than pure metals

Examples: Steel (iron + carbon), Bronze (copper + tin), Brass (copper + zinc)

Key Points to Remember

  • Metallic bonding = positive ions + sea of delocalised electrons
  • Delocalised electrons explain conductivity
  • Sliding layers explain malleability
  • Alloys are harder because layers can't slide easily
