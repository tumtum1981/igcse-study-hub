State Changes and Diffusion

Summary

Changes of state occur when substances change between solid, liquid, and gas. These changes involve energy transfer and can be explained using the particle model. Diffusion is another process explained by kinetic theory - particles spread from areas of high concentration to low concentration due to their random movement.

Key Definitions

Term Definition
Melting Change from solid to liquid when heated
Boiling Change from liquid to gas at the boiling point (throughout the liquid)
Evaporation Change from liquid to gas at temperatures below boiling point (at the surface only)
Condensation Change from gas to liquid when cooled
Freezing Change from liquid to solid when cooled
Sublimation Change directly from solid to gas (without becoming liquid)
Diffusion The spreading out of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration

Particle Diagrams

State Particle Arrangement Particle Movement Forces Between Particles
Solid Regular pattern, touching, close together Vibrate in fixed positions Strong forces hold particles in place
Liquid Random arrangement, touching, close together Move around each other, slide past Weaker forces, particles can move
Gas Random arrangement, far apart Move quickly in all directions Very weak forces, particles are free

Energy Changes During State Changes

Change of State Energy Change What Happens to Particles
Melting Energy absorbed (endothermic) Particles gain energy, overcome some forces, start moving around each other
Boiling Energy absorbed (endothermic) Particles gain enough energy to completely break free from other particles
Condensation Energy released (exothermic) Particles lose energy, slow down, forces pull them together
Freezing Energy released (exothermic) Particles lose energy, slow down, lock into fixed positions

Heating and Cooling Curves

During a change of state:

Temperature remains CONSTANT

Energy is used to break/form bonds between particles, NOT to change temperature

  • On a heating curve, flat sections show melting and boiling points
  • Sloped sections show temperature increasing as particles gain kinetic energy
  • Melting point = freezing point (same temperature)
  • Boiling point = condensation point (same temperature)

Evaporation vs Boiling

Feature Evaporation Boiling
Temperature Any temperature below boiling point Only at boiling point
Where it occurs Surface of liquid only Throughout the liquid
Bubbles No bubbles Bubbles form
Speed Slow Fast

Melting and Boiling Points of Common Substances

Substance Melting Point (°C) Boiling Point (°C) State at 25°C
Water 0 100 Liquid
Ethanol -114 78 Liquid
Bromine -7 59 Liquid
Oxygen -219 -183 Gas
Gallium 30 2204 Solid

To work out the state of a substance at a given temperature: if the temperature is below the melting point, it is a solid. If it is between the melting and boiling points, it is a liquid. If it is above the boiling point, it is a gas.

Diffusion

Diffusion is the spreading out of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration. It happens because particles are constantly moving in random directions.

Diffusion and Kinetic Theory

  • All particles are in constant random motion (this is kinetic theory)
  • Because particles move randomly, they gradually spread out from where there are lots of them (high concentration) to where there are fewer (low concentration)
  • Diffusion continues until the particles are evenly spread out (equilibrium)
  • No energy input is needed - diffusion happens on its own due to random particle movement

Factors Affecting the Rate of Diffusion

Factor Effect on Diffusion Rate Explanation
Temperature Higher temperature = faster diffusion Particles have more kinetic energy and move faster
Concentration difference Bigger difference = faster diffusion More particles moving from high to low concentration area
State of matter Faster in gases than liquids Gas particles move faster and are further apart (no diffusion in solids)

Examples of Diffusion

  • Smell spreading: Perfume particles diffuse through the air from high concentration (near the bottle) to low concentration (across the room)
  • Tea bag in water: Colour particles diffuse from the tea bag (high concentration) into the surrounding water (low concentration)
  • Bromine gas: Brown bromine gas in a gas jar diffuses into a jar of air until both jars are evenly brown

Key Points to Remember

  • State changes are physical changes (reversible)
  • No new substances are formed during state changes
  • Temperature stays constant during melting and boiling
  • Energy is needed to overcome forces between particles
  • Particles in a gas have the most energy, particles in a solid have the least
  • Diffusion is the net movement of particles from high to low concentration
  • Diffusion is faster at higher temperatures and in gases compared to liquids
