Covalent Bonding

Summary

A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. This type of bonding typically occurs between non-metal atoms. The shared electrons are attracted to both nuclei, holding the atoms together.

How Covalent Bonds Form

  • Non-metal atoms need to gain electrons to achieve a full outer shell
  • Instead of transferring electrons, they share them
  • Each shared pair of electrons forms one covalent bond
  • The shared electrons are attracted to both positive nuclei

Simple Covalent Molecules

Molecule Formula Bonds Shared Pairs
Hydrogen H2 Single bond 1 pair
Water H2O 2 single bonds 2 pairs
Oxygen O2 Double bond 2 pairs
Nitrogen N2 Triple bond 3 pairs
Methane CH4 4 single bonds 4 pairs
Carbon dioxide CO2 2 double bonds 4 pairs

Properties of Simple Covalent Molecules

Property Explanation
Low melting/boiling points Weak forces between molecules (intermolecular forces) - easy to overcome
Do not conduct electricity No free electrons or ions to carry charge
Often gases or liquids at room temperature Weak intermolecular forces

Important: The covalent bonds within molecules are strong - it's the forces between molecules that are weak!

Key Points to Remember

  • Covalent bonds form by sharing electrons
  • Occurs between non-metals
  • Single bond = 1 shared pair, double = 2, triple = 3
  • Simple molecular substances have low melting points
  • They don't conduct electricity
