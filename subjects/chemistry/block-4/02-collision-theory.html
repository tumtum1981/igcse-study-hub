Collision Theory and Rates of Reaction

Collision Theory

For a chemical reaction to occur, the reacting particles must collide with each other. But not every collision leads to a reaction. Collision theory states that:

  • Particles must collide with sufficient energy (at or above the activation energy)
  • Particles must collide with the correct orientation

A collision that results in a reaction is called an effective collision (or successful collision).

KEY TERM

Activation energy is the minimum amount of energy particles must have when they collide in order for a reaction to occur.

Rate of Reaction

The rate of reaction tells us how quickly reactants are converted into products. A faster rate means more product is formed per unit of time.

FORMULA

Rate of reaction = amount of reactant used (or product formed) ÷ time

The rate of reaction depends on the frequency of effective collisions. Anything that increases the number of successful collisions per second will increase the rate.

Factors Affecting Rate of Reaction

1. Concentration (solutions)

Increasing the concentration of a dissolved reactant increases the rate of reaction.

  • Higher concentration means more particles in the same volume
  • Particles are closer together and collide more frequently
  • More collisions per second → more successful collisions → faster reaction

2. Pressure (gases)

Increasing the pressure of a gas reaction increases the rate in the same way as increasing concentration.

  • Higher pressure squeezes gas particles into a smaller volume
  • Particles are closer together and collide more frequently

3. Surface Area (solids)

Breaking a solid into smaller pieces (or using a powder instead of a lump) increases the surface area exposed to the other reactant.

  • More surface area = more particles exposed and available to react
  • Collision frequency increases → faster rate of reaction
  • Example: powdered marble reacts much faster with acid than marble chips

4. Temperature

Increasing temperature increases the rate of reaction.

  • Particles gain more kinetic energy and move faster
  • Collisions happen more frequently
  • A greater proportion of particles have energy at or above the activation energy
  • Both effects together lead to significantly more successful collisions per second

A useful rule of thumb: for many reactions, increasing temperature by 10°C approximately doubles the rate of reaction.

5. Catalysts

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being used up or permanently changed.

  • A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy
  • More particles have energy above the lower activation energy → more successful collisions
  • The catalyst is not consumed and can be reused
  • Example: manganese(IV) oxide as a catalyst for the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide

Summary Table

Concentration / Pressure Increase Faster More particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions
Surface area Increase (smaller pieces) Faster More particles exposed → more frequent collisions
Temperature Increase Faster More kinetic energy → more frequent and more energetic collisions
