Concentration and Analysis of Graphs

Reading Rate of Reaction Graphs

Graphs of volume of gas produced (cm³) against time (s) are commonly used to show the progress of a reaction. Being able to read and interpret these graphs is an essential exam skill.

Key observations from rate graphs:

  • Steeper initial gradient = faster initial rate of reaction
  • All curves reach the same final volume = same amount of product (same limiting reagent used)
  • Higher concentration curve reaches the plateau sooner = reaction finishes faster
  • Gradient decreases over time = rate slows as reactants are used up

Calculating Rate from a Graph

The rate at any point on a volume-time graph equals the gradient of the tangent at that point.

FORMULA

Rate = Δ volume ÷ Δ time (gradient of tangent)
Units: cm³/s or cm³/min

For initial rate: draw a tangent at t = 0 (the start of the curve) and calculate its gradient.

Worked example: A tangent drawn at t = 0 on a rate curve goes from (0, 0) to (20 s, 40 cm³).

Initial rate = 40 cm³ ÷ 20 s = 2 cm³/s

Effect of Concentration on Rate vs Total Amount

An important distinction for exams:

Change Effect on Rate Effect on Total Amount of Product
Higher concentration (same volume, more concentrated) Faster rate (steeper gradient) More product (more reactant available)
More dilute concentration (same volume, less concentrated) Slower rate (gentler gradient) Less product (less reactant available)
Same concentration, same volume (repeat) Same rate Same total product — curves should overlap
Smaller marble chips (same mass, same concentration) Faster rate (more surface area) Same total product (same mass of marble)

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [2 marks]

On a graph of volume of CO₂ against time, a tangent drawn at t = 0 passes through the points (0, 0) and (30 s, 60 cm³). Calculate the initial rate of reaction.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Rate = Δ volume ÷ Δ time = 60 ÷ 30 = 2 cm³/s (1 mark for correct method, 1 mark for answer with units)
Question 2 [3 marks]

Two experiments use the same mass of marble chips with hydrochloric acid. In Experiment A, small chips are used; in Experiment B, large chips are used. The same concentration and volume of acid is used in both. Describe the expected differences between the two curves on a volume–time graph.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Experiment A (small chips) has a steeper initial gradient — faster rate due to greater surface area (1 mark)
  • Both curves reach the same final volume of CO₂ because the same mass of marble (same moles of CaCO₃) is used (1 mark)
  • Experiment A reaches the final volume sooner (shorter reaction time) (1 mark)
Question 3 [2 marks]

A student repeats an experiment with twice the volume of hydrochloric acid at the same concentration. Describe how the graph would differ from the original.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • The final volume of CO₂ would be higher (double) because twice as many moles of HCl are available (1 mark)
  • The initial gradient (rate) would be similar to the original — concentration is the same, so collision frequency is the same (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

Explain why the gradient of a volume–time rate graph decreases as the reaction proceeds.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • As the reaction proceeds, reactants are used up, so their concentration decreases (1 mark)
  • Fewer collisions occur per second, so the rate (and therefore the gradient) decreases over time (1 mark)
Question 5 [4 marks]

A catalyst is added to an experiment with marble chips and hydrochloric acid. Predict and explain the effect on: (a) the initial rate of reaction, and (b) the total volume of CO₂ produced.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • (a) Initial rate increases (steeper gradient) (1 mark) because the catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy, so more collisions are successful (1 mark)
  • (b) Total volume of CO₂ stays the same (1 mark) because a catalyst does not change the amount of reactants — the same mass of CaCO₃ and volume of HCl still react (1 mark)
