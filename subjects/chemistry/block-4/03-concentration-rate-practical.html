Concentration and Rate of Reaction Practical

Measuring Rate of Reaction

The rate of reaction can be measured in several ways depending on what is produced or consumed during the reaction:

  • Volume of gas produced per unit time (e.g. using a gas syringe or measuring cylinder over water)
  • Mass lost per unit time (e.g. if a gas escapes, the mass decreases)
  • Change in colour or turbidity (e.g. the sodium thiosulfate and hydrochloric acid reaction)
  • Change in pH using a data logger
FORMULA

Rate = amount of product formed (or reactant used) ÷ time taken

The Sodium Thiosulfate Practical

One of the most common rate of reaction practicals uses sodium thiosulfate solution reacting with hydrochloric acid.

WORD EQUATION

sodium thiosulfate + hydrochloric acid → sodium chloride + sulfur + sulfur dioxide + water

A yellow precipitate of sulfur forms during this reaction, making the solution turn cloudy. The rate of reaction is measured by timing how long it takes for the cloudiness to obscure a cross drawn on paper underneath the flask.

Method

  • Place a conical flask on a piece of paper with a cross drawn on it
  • Add a measured volume of sodium thiosulfate solution to the flask
  • Add a measured volume of hydrochloric acid and start the stopwatch
  • Look down through the solution at the cross
  • Stop the timer when the cross can no longer be seen through the cloudy solution
  • Record the time; calculate rate as 1/time (in s-1)

Investigating the Effect of Concentration

To investigate how concentration affects rate, the concentration of sodium thiosulfate is varied while keeping all other variables constant.

Variables

Variable type Variable How controlled/measured
Independent Concentration of sodium thiosulfate Varied by diluting with different volumes of water; measure with a measuring cylinder
Dependent Rate of reaction (1/time) Measure time in seconds until cross disappears; calculate 1/time
Controlled Volume and concentration of HCl Use same volume from the same stock solution each time
Controlled Temperature Carry out all experiments at room temperature
Controlled Total volume of solution Keep total volume constant by adjusting the volume of water added

Marble Chips and Hydrochloric Acid

Another common practical uses marble chips (calcium carbonate) reacting with hydrochloric acid. This is used to investigate the effect of concentration or surface area on rate.

WORD EQUATION

calcium carbonate + hydrochloric acid → calcium chloride + water + carbon dioxide

Measuring the Rate

  • Method 1 - Gas volume: Collect CO2 in a gas syringe; record volume every 30 seconds; rate = volume/time
  • Method 2 - Mass loss: Place flask on a balance; record mass every 30 seconds; CO2 escaping causes the mass to decrease; rate = mass lost/time

Drawing and Interpreting Rate Graphs

  • A steeper initial gradient means a faster rate of reaction
  • The curve levels off (becomes horizontal) when a reactant is used up
  • All experiments using the same amount of limiting reactant reach the same final value - just at different speeds
  • A higher concentration gives a steeper curve that levels off at the same final point

Evaluating the Practical

Issue Effect Improvement
Judging when cross disappears is subjective Different observers may stop the timer at different points Use a light sensor/colorimeter to measure turbidity objectively
Room temperature may change Temperature affects rate - could be a confounding variable Use a water bath to control temperature
Mixing may not be consistent Different swirling speeds could affect the rate Use a magnetic stirrer set to a fixed speed

* IGCSE Practice Questions

Question 1 [3 marks]

Describe how you would investigate the effect of concentration on the rate of the sodium thiosulfate and hydrochloric acid reaction. Include how you would measure the rate.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Vary the concentration of sodium thiosulfate (e.g. by diluting with water) while keeping volume of HCl, temperature, and total volume constant (1 mark)
  • Place the flask over a paper with a cross; add acid and start the timer (1 mark)
  • Record the time for the cross to disappear; calculate rate as 1/time (1 mark)
Question 2 [2 marks]

In a sodium thiosulfate experiment, the time recorded was 50 seconds. Calculate the rate and give the unit.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Rate = 1/time = 1/50 = 0.02 (1 mark)
  • Unit: s⁻¹ (per second) (1 mark)
Question 3 [2 marks]

A student investigates the reaction of marble chips with hydrochloric acid by measuring mass loss over time. Sketch what a graph of mass loss vs time would look like for two different concentrations of acid (label the curves).

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Both curves should level off at the same final mass loss (same amount of marble used) (1 mark)
  • The higher concentration curve should have a steeper initial gradient and reach the plateau sooner (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

State one limitation of using the disappearing cross method and suggest an improvement.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Limitation: judging when the cross disappears is subjective / different observers may stop the timer at different points (1 mark)
  • Improvement: use a colorimeter or light sensor to measure turbidity objectively (1 mark)
Question 5 [3 marks]

Write the word equation for the reaction of marble chips with hydrochloric acid. State two methods that could be used to measure the rate of this reaction.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • calcium carbonate + hydrochloric acid → calcium chloride + water + carbon dioxide (1 mark)
  • Any two from: measure volume of CO₂ collected in gas syringe over time / measure decrease in mass of flask over time as CO₂ escapes / measure time for a set volume of CO₂ to be produced (1 mark each)
