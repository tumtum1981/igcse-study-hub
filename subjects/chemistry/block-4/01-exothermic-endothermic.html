Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

Exothermic Reactions

KEY TERM

An exothermic reaction is a type of chemical reaction in which energy is transferred to the surroundings from the reactants. The temperature of the surroundings increases.

Examples of exothermic reactions:

Reaction Type Example Equation
Combustion Burning methane CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)
Neutralisation Strong acid + strong base HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)
Displacement Silver nitrate + copper 2AgNO3(aq) + Cu(s) → 2Ag(s) + Cu(NO3)2(aq)

Note: Most neutralisation reactions are exothermic. However, reactions involving weak acids (e.g. citric acid) and weak bases (e.g. sodium hydrogencarbonate) may absorb energy overall, making them endothermic.

Energy Dissipation After Exothermic Reactions

After an exothermic reaction is complete, the energy dissipates into the environment from the reaction mixture. As the environment is very large, the energy increase has only a very small impact on the room temperature. The reaction solution will cool back to room temperature.

Everyday uses:

  • Hand warmers - chemical reaction transfers energy to warm your hands
  • Self-heating meals and cans - food is heated by the exothermic reaction

Endothermic Reactions

KEY TERM

An endothermic reaction is a type of chemical reaction in which energy from the surroundings is transferred to the reaction. The temperature of the surroundings decreases.

Examples of endothermic reactions:

Reaction Type Example Equation
Photosynthesis Plants making glucose 6CO2(g) + 6H2O(l) → C6H12O6(s) + 6O2(g)
Electrolysis Decomposition of copper chloride CuCl2(l) → Cu(s) + Cl2(g)
Thermal decomposition Heating copper carbonate CuCO3(s) → CuO(s) + CO2(g)

After an endothermic reaction, energy from the environment transfers to the reaction solution, warming it back to room temperature.

Everyday uses:

  • Self-cooling drinks - energy absorbed from the drink cools it down
  • Sports injury cool packs - energy absorbed from the injured body part

Comparing Exothermic and Endothermic

Exothermic Endothermic
Energy transfer Reaction → surroundings Surroundings → reaction
Temperature change Surroundings heat up Surroundings cool down
Examples Combustion, neutralisation, displacement Photosynthesis, electrolysis, thermal decomposition
Everyday use Hand warmers, self-heating meals Cool packs, self-cooling drinks

Measuring Energy Changes: Calorimetry

The energy changes in exothermic and endothermic reactions can be measured using temperature. This method is called calorimetry.

CALCULATION

Temperature change = final temperature − initial temperature

e.g. 39°C − 22°C = 17°C

Equipment needed:

  • Beaker
  • Insulated polystyrene cup with lid
  • Thermometer or temperature probe
  • Stirring rod
  • Reactants

For exothermic reactions, measure the initial and highest temperature reached. For endothermic reactions, measure the initial and lowest temperature reached.

Question 1 [2 marks]

Define the term 'exothermic reaction'.

  • A reaction in which energy is transferred to the surroundings (1 mark)
  • The temperature of the surroundings increases (1 mark)
Question 2 [2 marks]

Give two examples of exothermic reactions.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Any two from: combustion, neutralisation, displacement reactions (1 mark each)
Question 3 [3 marks]

Explain why a sports injury cool pack feels cold when activated.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • The cool pack contains chemicals that undergo an endothermic reaction (1 mark)
  • Energy is transferred from the surroundings (the injured body part) to the reaction (1 mark)
  • This causes the temperature of the surroundings to decrease, so it feels cold (1 mark)
Question 4 [2 marks]

After an exothermic reaction is complete, the reaction solution cools back to room temperature. Explain why.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Energy dissipates from the reaction mixture into the environment (1 mark)
  • The environment is very large so the energy increase has only a small impact on room temperature (1 mark)
Question 5 [2 marks]

In a calorimetry experiment, the initial temperature was 21°C and the highest temperature reached was 38°C. Calculate the temperature change and state whether this was an exothermic or endothermic reaction.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Temperature change = 38 − 21 = 17°C (1 mark)
  • Exothermic, because the temperature increased (1 mark)
