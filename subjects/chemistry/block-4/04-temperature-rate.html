Temperature and Rate of Reaction

How Temperature Affects Rate

Temperature has a significant effect on the rate of chemical reactions. Increasing the temperature increases the rate of reaction.

Using collision theory:

  • More kinetic energy: particles move faster at higher temperatures
  • More frequent collisions: faster-moving particles collide more often per second
  • More energetic collisions: a higher proportion of collisions have energy equal to or greater than the activation energy
  • Both effects together mean many more successful collisions per second → faster rate
KEY RULE

As a rough guide, for many reactions: rate approximately doubles for every 10°C rise in temperature.

Activation Energy and Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

Not all particles in a sample have the same energy. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shows the spread of energies among particles at a given temperature.

  • The activation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy needed for a collision to result in a reaction
  • At low temperature, few particles have energy ≥ Ea → slow reaction
  • At high temperature, many more particles have energy ≥ Ea → fast reaction
  • The area under the curve to the right of the Ea line represents the fraction of successful collisions

Investigating the Effect of Temperature

The sodium thiosulfate and hydrochloric acid disappearing cross experiment can be used to investigate temperature:

Variable Detail
Independent Temperature of sodium thiosulfate solution (e.g. 20, 30, 40, 50, 60°C)
Dependent Time for cross to disappear (then calculate rate = 1/time)
Controlled Concentration of both reactants, volume of solutions, size of cross, observer

Method: Heat sodium thiosulfate to the required temperature in a water bath. Add HCl and start timing. Stop when the cross is no longer visible. Calculate rate = 1/time. Plot rate vs temperature.

Expected results: as temperature increases, time decreases and rate (1/time) increases. A graph of rate vs temperature should show an upward curve.

Temperature and Rate: Graph Interpretation

On a graph of rate (1/time) against temperature:

  • The line curves upward — rate increases more steeply as temperature rises
  • This reflects the exponential relationship between temperature and the number of particles exceeding activation energy
  • Unlike concentration (roughly linear), the relationship with temperature is non-linear

On a graph of volume of gas against time at different temperatures:

  • Higher temperature → steeper initial gradient (faster rate) but the same final volume (same amount of product if limiting reagent is unchanged)
  • The curve at higher temperature reaches the plateau earlier

Define activation energy and state how raising the temperature affects the proportion of particles that exceed it.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Activation energy is the minimum energy required for a collision to result in a reaction (1 mark)
  • Raising the temperature increases the proportion of particles with energy equal to or greater than the activation energy (1 mark)
Question 3 [2 marks]

A reaction takes 80 seconds at 20°C. Using the rule that rate doubles per 10°C rise, estimate the time for the reaction at 40°C.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Rate doubles for each 10°C rise; 20°C → 40°C is a 20°C increase, so rate doubles twice (×4) (1 mark)
  • If rate is 4× faster, time = 80 ÷ 4 = 20 seconds (1 mark)
Question 4 [3 marks]

Describe how you would investigate the effect of temperature on the rate of the sodium thiosulfate and HCl reaction, stating the variables clearly.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Independent variable: temperature of sodium thiosulfate (e.g. heat to different temperatures using a water bath) (1 mark)
  • Dependent variable: time for cross to disappear / rate = 1/time (1 mark)
  • Controlled variables: concentrations of both solutions, volumes, same cross, same observer — keep all constant to ensure a fair test (1 mark)
Question 5 [3 marks]

Two curves are drawn on a graph of volume of CO₂ (cm³) against time for marble chips + HCl. Curve A is at 20°C and Curve B is at 40°C. Describe the differences between the two curves and explain them.

* CLICK TO REVEAL ANSWER
  • Curve B (40°C) has a steeper initial gradient than Curve A, meaning the reaction is faster at higher temperature (1 mark)
  • Both curves reach the same final volume of CO₂ because the same amount of limiting reagent is used (1 mark)
  • Curve B reaches the final volume in less time because more collisions have energy ≥ activation energy at higher temperature (1 mark)
